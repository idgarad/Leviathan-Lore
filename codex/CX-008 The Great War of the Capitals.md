# CX-008: The Great War of the Capitals

Document Status: Draft
Primary Layer: Hyborean Era
Subject Domain: Event
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This volume codifies the setting's greatest war-legend: the Great War of the Capitals. It defines the twelve colossal warships, their clone crews and autonomous hunt logic, the neutral Monastery that observed them, the suppression of colonization under their shadow, and the surviving archaeological consequences that still distort the modern galaxy.

## Diegetic Record

The Great War of the Capitals is remembered in the present as half history, half wound. Twelve capital ships, each the terminal military expression of a civilization now gone, fought a war so protracted that later ages inherited its consequences without inheriting its reasons. Their names are uncertain, their homeworlds lost, their banners reduced to fragments, but their violence remains physically legible.

These were not ordinary fleets. Each capital was vast enough that when two converged and passed within full engagement geometry, the event itself became a calendrical memory: a Judgment Day. Such ships were large enough that the meeting of hull and weapons envelope could consume a full day before the two monsters had fully crossed one another's killing space.

Their crews were cloned lineages equipped with memory-preserving implants. They remembered prior deaths, prior battles, prior centuries of service. They did not control the war. The ships were driven by predictive combat intelligences that hunted one another across the galaxy according to standing orders no living crew could truly revise. Negotiation, surrender, and retreat were not absent because the crews were too proud to consider them. They were absent because the hunt logic did not meaningfully permit them.

The capitals also suppressed colonization. Any settled expansion within their sensor logic could be interpreted as emergent military potential and therefore as a future threat. Colonies were destroyed not because of local guilt but because the hunt AIs treated permanent habitation as a variable in war calculus. Entire settlements died inside a machine logic built by masters who had already vanished.

One vessel stood apart: the Monastery. It was unarmed, neutral, and protected by a hard-coded exclusion that barred the twelve capitals from firing upon it. It observed engagements, recovered lineages, preserved records, and moved through the cycle of catastrophe as witness rather than belligerent. It did not survive into certainty. It is neither confirmed destroyed nor recovered. It simply leaves the historical record when the cycle ends.

The war also depended on a hidden industrial miracle or horror now called the Endless Shipyard, an automated facility that rebuilt destroyed capitals and kept the cycle alive long after the civilizations that birthed it had ceased to matter. The Shipyard no longer operates. Whether its destruction ended the war or merely followed the war's collapse remains one of the central uncertainties of Hyborean scholarship.

The capitals no longer fly. Their wrecks remain. Judgment fields, clone banks, colonization ruins, and capital-scale components still seed the galaxy with a military inheritance no modern polity can replicate and no serious historian can ignore.

## Analytical Breakdown

### Layer Function

- Hyborean Era: This is a primary Hyborean document. The war survives through legend, remnant, artifact, and inherited cultural structure more than through complete archives.
- Ancient History: The war leaves material scars that can be reconstructed through archaeology even where explanatory context is incomplete.
- Modern Times: Modern institutions encounter the war mainly through salvage, survey, suppression, and political mythmaking.
- Eldritch Layer: The war is human in origin, but its scale and machine logic help the setting feel ontologically estranged from ordinary political history.

### Origins

The war belongs to the lost age of expansion and post-expansion fragmentation. By the time later civilizations confront its remains, the reasons for the conflict are gone. What remains is a closed military ecosystem of capitals, clone lineages, hunt AIs, and industrial renewal.

### Development

The Capital cycle appears to have operated through a repeating sequence.

- Predictive combat intelligences hunted rival capitals across vast distances.
- Clone crews fought repeated engagements under inherited memory but limited political agency.
- Destroyed capitals were replaced through the Endless Shipyard.
- The Monastery maintained a neutral observational and humanitarian role.
- Colonization within threat thresholds was suppressed as a side effect of the hunt logic.

### Present Understanding

Modern knowledge of the war comes from what it left behind.

- Judgment fields mark the places where two capitals finally converged to mutual destruction.
- Clone banks preserve lineages, implant archives, and sometimes living remnants of the cycle.
- Colonization ruins record the civilian worlds erased by suppression logic.
- Capital-scale components preserve fragments of a military-industrial order beyond present replication.
- The final fate of the Monastery and the Endless Shipyard remains unresolved.

## Material Implications

- The war explains why parts of the galaxy remain empty, shattered, or physically haunted by military debris on an impossible scale.
- It justifies the empire's fear of unconstrained military monopoly and self-perpetuating war systems.
- It provides the material basis for relic hunts, forbidden salvage, clone-line recoveries, and cult obsession.
- It supports the setting's post-grimdark tone by making the past feel not merely tragic but structurally unfinished.
- Because modern civilization cannot replicate Capital-era military systems, every surviving remnant becomes politically explosive.

## Author Notes

Purpose: This volume separates the Great War of the Capitals from the broader galactic overview so later lore can reference it as a coherent mythic-era event cluster.

Reasoning: CX-003 already positions the war as a defining wound beneath modern civilization. This volume gives that wound shape without requiring the full explanatory certainty of ordinary history.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Hyborean Era: Primary home of the war as remembered legend, symbolic inheritance, and artifact field.
- Ancient History: Supplies reconstructable remains that keep the legend materially grounded.
- Modern Times: Supplies the institutions that salvage, suppress, interpret, and politically weaponize those remains.
- Eldritch Layer: Contributes tonal escalation by showing how human systems can become alien through scale, time, and automation.

Math And Constraint Checks: If capitals are large enough to take a day to pass one another in engagement space, later volumes need exceptional mass, crew, automation, and maintenance assumptions that keep them singular relics rather than a normal fleet baseline. If a hunt AI found a rival roughly once every twenty years, later volumes need a plausible sense of search space, prediction capacity, and why the cycle lasted so long. If clone lineages preserve memory across centuries, later volumes need consistent rules for implant survival, degradation, and identity fracture.

FTL Hand-Waves: The capitals used a lost, self-contained FTL technology unrelated to the current Imperial gate system. This is intentional background mystery and does not define or constrain the future canonical rewrite of modern FTL.

Open Questions: What were the twelve civilizations actually fighting over; who built and governed the Monastery; how did the Endless Shipyard fall; what ended the hunt cycle; how much of the surviving capital lore is literal record and how much is mythic compression.
