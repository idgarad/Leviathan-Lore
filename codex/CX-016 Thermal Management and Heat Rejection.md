# CX-016: Thermal Management and Heat Rejection

Document Status: Draft
Primary Layer: Modern Times
Subject Domain: Technology
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This volume defines the critical systems responsible for managing and rejecting waste heat in Leviathan vessels: **Thermal Batteries** and **Heat Exchangers**. It details their physical properties, operational principles, and how they serve as the central thermal sink for FTL operations, shield systems, and weapon discharge. The volume emphasizes the strategic importance of thermal management—establishing that **Thermal Batteries are the true "Hit Points" of a vessel**—as a primary constraint on both offensive and defensive capabilities, reinforcing the "Math Holds" axiom.

## Diegetic Record

In the cold vacuum of space, heat is not merely a byproduct; it is a weapon, a liability, and a fundamental constraint on all high-energy operations. Every active system, from the smallest life support unit to the most powerful FTL engine or energy weapon, generates waste heat. Without a robust and efficient means of managing this thermal load, a vessel quickly becomes a molten coffin.

The Imperial solution to this omnipresent challenge lies in a three-stage cooling architecture: **Thermal Batteries** for storage, **Heat Exchangers** for transfer, and **External Radiators** for ultimate rejection. These are not mere auxiliary components; they are the circulatory system of a ship's endurance, dictating everything from jump cadence to combat duration. A critical engineering distinction is that while the primary machinery handling xM must be built to withstand unique CHJ forces, the external radiators do not interact with xM at all; they operate on entirely separate coolant loops.

Thermal Batteries are the ship's primary heat sinks. They are designed to rapidly absorb and store massive quantities of thermal energy, acting as a buffer against instantaneous heat spikes. Whether from the violent friction of an FTL jump, the conversion of incoming weapon energy by shields, or the self-inflicted thermal recoil of a beam weapon, all excess heat eventually finds its way into these batteries. Their capacity defines a ship's "thermal headroom"—the amount of punishment it can absorb or dish out before its systems begin to fail.

Heat Exchangers act as the critical interface between the static batteries and active coolant loops. They transfer stored energy out of the batteries and into a circulating secondary medium. This heated coolant is then pumped to the hull’s exterior radiators. Only the heat exchangers on the xD (Exotic Discharge) hot lines are "heavy duty," as they must handle the internal CHJ forces (xM lag) generated by the active matter. The rest of the ship's heat exchangers, secondary loops, and radiators do not deal with xM and use standard industrial engineering.

Together, these systems ensure that every action in the void has a thermal consequence. In the brutal reality of space combat, managing that consequence is more vital than the integrity of the hull; a ship usually cooks its crew long before it is physically destroyed.

## Thermal Architecture Sizing Logic

In accordance with the "Math Holds" axiom, shipboard thermal systems are sized sequentially to ensure they can handle the peak loads generated by FTL and combat.
Multiple batteries are utilized to meet ship-wide capacity and redundancy requirements.

### Stock Scaling Laws
To maintain engineering parity across all ship classes, thermal components follow the standard linear scale factor ($k$) relative to the Falcon-class (Small) baseline.

*   **Capacity Scaling (Volume-Based):** Component storage capacity (MJ) scales with $k^3$.
    *   $B_{th}(k) = B_{th,ref} \times k^3$
*   **Transfer Scaling (Area-Based):** Heat exchanger and radiator throughput (MW) scales with $k^2$.
    *   $P_{X}(k) = P_{X,ref} \times k^2$
    *   $Q_{cool}(k) = Q_{cool,ref} \times k^2$

### 1. Stage One: Interface Architecture (The Ports)
Thermal energy enters the batteries through dedicated ports on the battery shell. These interfaces are color-coded based on their thermal throughput rating:

*   **Black Interface (Heavy):** Designed for high-intensity, transient thermal loads (primarily xD FTL discharge).
*   **White Interface (Light):** Designed for constant-flow, lower-wattage loads (ECS, Shields, Armor).
*   **Thermal Flux Limits ($\phi_q$):** To ensure hardware longevity and prevent coolant cavitation, Heavy Input exchangers are designed for a peak flux of **0.5 to 1.5 MW/m²**. 

### 2. Equipment Integration: The Exchanger Plug
Heat exchangers are distinct equipment modules that "bolt into" the battery interfaces.

*   **The Thermal Plug:** Every heat exchanger is equipped with a high-conductivity "Plug" (White or Black) that inserts through the battery's insulation shell to contact the Lithium-Fluoride (LiF) core.
*   **Insulation Backing:** The plug hardware features its own integrated insulation backing. When bolted in, this backing restores the integrity of the battery's vacuum-gap shell.
*   **The Radiant Flash Hazard:** Removing a heat exchanger plug from a "Hot" battery (any unit above 5% saturation) is a lethal industrial hazard.
    *   **Lethality:** Disconnecting a plug exposes the 1,100 K radiant core. The resulting thermal release will flash-incinerate personnel and melt bulkheads in the compartment almost instantly.
    *   **Deadbolt Interlocks:** Standard safety protocols require physical deadbolts that prevent the unbolting of any plug while the battery core is above handling temperature.

### 3. Internal Loop Manifolds and Return Paths
From a management interface perspective, the ship's thermal architecture is organized into discrete **Thermal Zones** managed via manifold aggregation.

#### 2.1 Intake and Load Balancing
Incoming thermal lines (HURL for xM, ECS for shields/life support) terminate into a **Primary Intake Manifold**.

*   **Light Loop Constraints (White):** 
    *   **Constant Flow:** The White loop is a constant-flow system.
    *   **Line Redundancy:** Ships utilize **dual lines** by default; losing one line reduces flow capacity by 50%.
    *   **Class Scaling:** Large-class hulls and above are mandated to use **quad-line** systems.
*   **Heavy Loop Constraints (Black):**
    *   **Transient Burst:** Designed specifically for the 60-second xD discharge window.

#### 3.2 Routing and Redundancy
*   **Manifold Cutovers:** Engineers can redirect flow between different battery units or redundant lines.
*   **Dirty Shunts:** In extreme emergencies, Light Input heat can be routed into Black (Heavy) exchangers. This risks hardware warping but can prevent a shield collapse.

#### 3.3 Output Normalization
*   **Load Balancing:** The manifold uses automated pressure-compensation valves to balance the thermal load across one or more active Heat Exchangers. This ensures that no single battery unit or exchanger face suffers "Thermal Pitting" from localized over-flux.
*   **N+1 Capability:** This manifold allows the system to isolate a failed exchanger or battery unit without shutting down the entire loop.

After passing through the heat exchangers and surrendering their energy to the battery PCM, the cooled fluids are collected by a **Primary Output Manifold**.
*   **Uniform Temperature:** The output manifold acts as a mixing chamber. Because the exchangers are drawing from a common thermal sink (the battery bank), the material exiting the manifold has a normalized, uniform temperature. This stability is critical for the downstream sensitive components.

#### 3.4 Return Logic by Loop Type
The return path for materials exiting the Primary Output Manifold is dictated by the loop's engineering requirements:

*   **Heavy Return (xM Loop):** 
    *   **The Discharge Requirement:** Even when cooled below $T_{reuse}$, xM cannot return to storage until its galactic imprint is reset. 
    *   **Path:** Output Manifold $\to$ **Discharge Module** $\to$ xM Storage Tanks / FTL Engine.
    *   This ensures the "tri-domain disagreement" is resolved before the matter is reintroduced to the active compression cycle.

*   **Light Return (ECS/Shield/Armor Loop):**
    *   **Continuous Feed:** Since the ECS side is a standard industrial constant-flow loop, the coolant does not require reconditioning.
    *   **Path:** Output Manifold $\to$ **Direct Return to Loop**.
    *   This provides the constant, stabilized thermal baseline required for life support and shield emitters to maintain field tension.

### 4. Stage Two: Thermal Battery Units (The Bottle)
Thermal Batteries are high-capacity, phase-change material storage units.

*   **Physical Nature:** Modern batteries utilize a **Lithium-Fluoride (LiF) Salt Lattice**. LiF has an exceptionally high latent heat of fusion ($\sim 1.05~\text{MJ/kg}$), allowing for compact storage.
*   **Construction (The Insulated Shell):** The bulk of a battery unit's volume is external insulation. The core is encased in a multi-layered **Vacuum-Gap Shell** and ceramic cladding to minimize conductive loss to the hull.
*   **Capacity ($B_{th}$):** Standardized to **2,000 MJ** for the Small (Falcon) class. Total capacity scales with $k^3$. While initially anchored to jump-heat safety margins, larger hulls gain significant "Thermal Endurance" due to volume-scaling outpacing heat-generation laws.
*   **Redundancy (The N+1 Doctrine):** All FTL-capable vessels require a minimum of **two independent thermal battery units** ($N+1$). The loss of a single battery in a single-unit system is considered a "Certain Death" scenario.
*   **Battery Models (Interface Configurations):**
    *   **Model WW (White-White):** Dual Light interfaces. Typically used for auxiliary-heavy or dedicated defensive (Shield/Armor) battery banks.
    *   **Model BB (Black-Black):** Dual Heavy interfaces. Reserved for high-performance FTL recovery banks or "Hot" combat ships utilizing plasma/beam dominance.
    *   **Model WB (White-Black):** One Light, one Heavy interface. The universal standard for general-purpose utility and mixed-role service.
*   **Volumetric Scaling:** To ensure adequate plumbing for heat exchangers, the physical volume of a thermal battery ($V_{bat}$) is anchored to **4x the volume of an FTL engine** of the same class.
*   **Volumetric Efficiency ($\eta_V$):** Approximately **13%** of the battery's volume is active PCM (salt), with the remaining volume dedicated to internal lattice support, heavy-duty xD manifolds, and coolant channels.
*   **Saturation:** As a thermal battery absorbs heat, its PCM transitions to a higher energy state. Once fully saturated (e.g., all PCM has melted), its ability to absorb further heat diminishes rapidly. Continued heat input beyond saturation leads to a rapid increase in system temperature, triggering safety shutdowns in connected systems (shields, weapons, FTL drives).

### 5. Stage Three: Outflow Heat Exchangers (The Cold Side)
The **Outflow Exchangers** facilitate the transfer of thermal energy from the batteries into the secondary coolant loops (e.g., liquid sodium or ammonia) running to the external radiators. 
*   **Physical Architecture:** To maximize thermal transfer efficiency and maintain internal symmetry, the **top and bottom surfaces** of each thermal battery unit are dedicated entirely to the outflow heat exchangers. This "sandwich" design allows the secondary coolant loops to draw heat from the largest available surface area of the PCM lattice.
*   **Sizing Requirement:** These units are sized to match the ship's **Active Rejection Rate** ($Q_{cool}$).
*   **Transfer Rate ($P_{out,X}$):** 
    $P_{out,X} = Q_{cool} = q_A \cdot A_{ship}$
*   **Outflow Flux:** Because $Q_{cool}$ is significantly lower than $P_{xD,X}$, the outflow exchangers operate at a very low flux ($\approx 0.1 \text{ MW/m²}$), ensuring high reliability for the primary cooling loop.

## Secondary Rejection: Disposable Thermal Ballast Modules (DTBM)

The DTBM is a separate, consumable heat-rejection system designed to remove entropy faster than passive radiation. It utilizes "disposable" thermal mass to bypass the bottleneck of external radiators.

### 1. The 50% Shunt Threshold
When the primary Thermal Batteries ($B_{th}$) reach **50% saturation**, the thermal management system acknowledges that heat generation is outpacing rejection. The ship automatically (or via manual override) opens a high-flow coolant line to the **DTBM cassettes**.

### 2. Operation and Dual-Use
The DTBM contains a dense, high-latent-heat ballast (typically lead-bismuth or specialized ceramic slurries). Once shunted, this ballast is superheated into a molten state, serving two tactical functions:
*   **Active Ejection (VFS-Bound):** The ship can physically eject the superheated ballast into the vacuum. 
    *   **The VFS Constraint:** In accordance with the Backstop Doctrine (see CX-015), ballast cannot be ejected without a **Valid Firing Solution**. Ejected molten metal is a multi-generational navigational hazard; it must terminate in a valid gravity well.
    *   **Tactical Delay:** Because a VFS is required, active ejection is **not a reactionary action**. In "Light" systems or complex navigational environments (like crowded stations), a captain may be forced to hold their heat for minutes or hours until a valid corridor opens.
    *   **Result:** A successful ejection provides an instantaneous drop in ship-wide thermal saturation.
*   **Plasma Feedstock:** As defined in CX-015, the molten ballast can be shunted to the Plasma Cannon array. The weapon uses the pre-heated mass as secondary propellant, converting the ship's internal waste heat into destructive kinetic and radiant energy.

### 3. Logistical Cost
Ejected or weaponized ballast is permanently lost. Every "slug" of ballast spent reduces the ship's emergency cooling capacity. Once the DTBM magazines are empty, the ship is restricted to its passive radiator $Q_{cool}$, making it highly vulnerable to thermal saturation and subsequent system shutdown.

### 4. Space Efficiency Trade-offs
The DTBM is significantly less space-efficient than the primary Thermal Battery ($B_{th}$). Because it requires heavy plumbing for molten shunting, cassette mechanicals, and ejection hardware, its "MJ per Cubic Meter" rating is much lower than the optimized LiF salt lattices of the primary sinks. Since internal ship volume is always at a premium, a DTBM installation represents a strategic sacrifice of habitable or cargo space in exchange for high-intensity thermal endurance.

## External Radiators (Drag-Tolerant Trunks)

Radiators are the final rejection point for all shipboard waste heat.

*   **Physical Nature:** External radiators are part of a secondary thermal loop that is physically isolated from the xM-bearing circuits. They contain standard industrial coolants and never encounter exotic matter.
*   **Design Doctrine:** While Imperial doctrine favors armored "trunks" or recessed panels to protect against combat damage and the fluid-like drag of CHJ space (see CX-013), they do not face the specialized internal structural loads associated with containing xM.
*   **Cooling Rate ($Q_{cool}$):** Measured in MegaWatts (MW). This rate is limited by the total radiator surface area ($A_{ship}$) and the material's emissivity ($q_A$).
*   **Vulnerability:** While armored, radiators are the "thermal signature" of the ship. Damaging these trunks prevents the ship from cooling, effectively trapping waste heat in the batteries and forcing an eventual tactical shutdown.

## Material Implications

### Tactical Doctrine: The Thermal HP
In the Leviathan setting, the traditional concept of "Hit Points" (Structural Integrity) is a secondary metric. The true fundamental HP of a ship is its **Thermal Battery Capacity**. 

*   **Cooking the Crew:** Modern weapons (Beams and Plasma) and the new combined deflector shield system are designed to maximize or manage thermal transfer. In a high-intensity engagement, the primary risk is rarely the physical disintegration of the hull, but rather the **saturation of the thermal batteries**. 
*   **Lethal Equilibrium:** Once batteries reach 100% saturation, the internal temperature of the vessel begins to climb uncontrollably. Even if the armor remains intact, the internal habitable zones quickly exceed the biological limits of the crew. Most surrenders or tactical retreats occur when the ship reaches "Critical Broil"—the point where life support can no longer isolate the crew from the radiant heat of the saturated core.

*   **Strategic Resource:** Thermal battery capacity and heat exchanger efficiency are as critical as weapon output or shield strength. A ship with superior thermal management can sustain combat longer or execute more rapid FTL maneuvers.
*   **Design Trade-offs:** Ship designers must balance thermal capacity and rejection against hull mass, volume, and survivability. Larger radiator trunks increase `A_ship` but also increase drag and potential target profile.
*   **Combat Endurance:** The "health" of a ship in combat is often less about its remaining hull integrity and more about its thermal battery saturation. The new shield system’s coupling and deflection mechanics mean that tactical choices (large strikes, light shots, missile barrages) directly affect how much heat is imparted to the batteries. A ship at 90% thermal capacity is severely limited in its offensive and defensive options.
*   **Maintenance:** Overloading thermal batteries or damaging heat exchangers can lead to permanent degradation, requiring extensive drydock maintenance at USO facilities (see CX-015).

## Author Notes

Purpose: To formalize the physics and mechanics of thermal management, which is a pervasive but undefined constraint in other volumes.

Reasoning: Thermal batteries and heat exchangers are mentioned as critical systems, but their specific operation and limitations were not explicitly detailed. This volume provides that necessary depth, reinforcing the "Math Holds" doctrine and providing a clear framework for tactical and engineering decisions.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Modern Times: Defines the current technological standard for thermal management.
- Ancient History: Provides a baseline against which older, less efficient, or lost thermal systems can be contrasted.
- Hyborean Era: Allows for the discovery of ancient thermal technologies with unknown properties.
- Eldritch Layer: Provides a framework for anomalous thermal phenomena or heat sources that defy conventional management.

Math And Constraint Checks: If thermal batteries have a finite capacity, later volumes need to define typical MJ capacities for different ship classes. If heat exchangers have a limited MW rejection rate, later volumes need to define typical rates and how they scale with `A_ship`. This volume provides the framework for those calculations.

FTL Hand-Waves: This volume accepts the FTL heat generation and xM cooling requirements from CX-013 without re-explaining FTL physics.

Open Questions: Specific MJ capacities for thermal batteries per ship class; specific MW rejection rates for heat exchangers per ship class; detailed material science of PCMs/molten salts used; specific failure modes for thermal batteries and exchangers under extreme stress.
