# AX-002: Lesser House Register

Document Status: Draft Appendix
Primary Layer: Modern Times
Subject Domain: Faction
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This appendix records the current public register of the lesser houses and their liege relationships. These houses do not hold major sovereign rank. They operate as bannermen, lease-holders, specialists, and governorship candidates beneath the major houses.

## Diegetic Record

The empire's feudal order is not sustained by the great houses alone. Beneath them is a dense secondary nobility of lesser houses: militant lineages, industrial operators, survey dynasts, contract lawyers, traders, auditors, archive keepers, and colonial specialists whose labor makes major-house ambition workable.

## Analytical Breakdown

### Registry Notes

- Lesser houses hold governorship scale rather than sector scale authority.
- Their standing depends on liege recognition, lease, charter, and performance.
- The register below groups them by current liege major house and records their dominant specialization.

## Material Implications

- The empire's feudal order becomes more granular and believable once the specialist lesser houses are visible.
- Bannerman networks explain how major houses scale beyond the capabilities of a single dynastic core.
- Lesser houses provide a natural source of governors, technical experts, and political intermediaries without flattening all capacity into the major houses themselves.

## Author Notes

Purpose: This appendix gives the empire's secondary nobility a structured public roster.

Reasoning: The setting needed a middle layer between great-house doctrine and anonymous background administration. Lesser houses provide that layer and make the feudal order feel inhabited rather than schematic.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Modern Times: Primary function as the active working nobility beneath the major houses.
- Ancient History: Some lesser houses likely ground their legitimacy in older service traditions and inherited professional roles.
- Hyborean Era: Certain houses consciously preserve archaic customs, heraldry, or ritual legitimacy to bolster present standing.
- Eldritch Layer: No direct registry role, though specific lesser houses may later prove important in archaeological, containment, or cult-response contexts.

Math And Constraint Checks: If 106 lesser houses meaningfully serve under 23 major houses, later volumes need plausible distributions of governorships, leased systems, staffing, and contract burden. If lesser houses are highly specialized, later lore should preserve those specializations rather than treating them as interchangeable generic nobility.

FTL Hand-Waves: None. References to navigation or unstable-route expertise are institutional, not explanatory.

Open Questions: Which lesser houses are oldest by record rather than myth; how often do lesser houses rise, collapse, or shift liege; what legal path allows a lesser house to become a major house by Imperial decree.
