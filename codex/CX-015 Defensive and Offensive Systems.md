# Energy Shield System

The energy shield system creates a protective bubble around the ship by projecting an energy-to-heat conversion field. This field is generated by a network of shield emitters embedded throughout the hull structure. Unlike FTL emitters, shield emitters are a standard, integral component of all ship hulls. As such, their presence does not require special architectural consideration during ship design or construction.

The shield system draws energy from the ship’s power grid to sustain the field. When the field is struck, it converts the kinetic energy of projectiles and the radiant energy of directed-energy weapons into heat.

### Thermal Integration

To prevent hardware failure during energy conversion, each shield emitter is serviced by a dedicated coolant loop. This loop circulates through the emitter assembly and carries the resulting thermal energy back to a primary heat exchanger.

The heat exchanger dumps this load into the vessel's **thermal batteries**—the same high-capacity storage units utilized by the FTL system (see CX-013) to manage jump-friction waste heat. 

This creates a **Thermal Drawdown System**: The "health" of the shield is the remaining capacity of the ship's thermal batteries. 

Maintaining the shield field also requires a continuous draw from the ship's power grid. Converting incoming energy to heat is an active process with a variable **Shield Efficiency ($\eta_{shield}$)**. 

As the ship's scale increases, the energy required to project and maintain the "tension" of the conversion bubble increases at a sublinear rate relative to volume. This causes the efficiency of the shield to decay as the vessel grows larger. For megastructures (World Ships, Titanic Battle-Stations), the efficiency approaches or drops below 1.0, meaning the power draw to sustain the field exceeds the energy being blocked—rendering energy shields structurally and energetically impractical for such vessels.

### Instantaneous Absorption Limit

While the thermal batteries handle the ship's total heat load, each emitter is constrained by the **Instantaneous Absorption Limit (IAL)**. This value functions as "Localized Hit Points" for a single strike. Because there is a finite volume of coolant present within an emitter's internal jackets, there is a maximum amount of energy (in MJ) that can be absorbed before the coolant vaporizes.

If an incoming strike delivers energy exceeding the emitter's current IAL, the shield absorbs energy up to that limit, and any excess energy "leaks" through the field to strike the hull armor directly.

*Tactical Note: Larger emitter classes (Large, Capital, Titanic) typically feature significantly higher coolant volumes, allowing them to absorb much larger single-instance energy spikes than Small or Medium units.*

### Sustainable Flow Rate (SFR)

The **Sustainable Flow Rate (SFR)** is the speed at which the shield "regenerates." It represents the rate (in MW or MJ/s) at which the pumps can cycle hot coolant out of the emitter and replace it with fresh, cool fluid from the primary loop.

If a ship's IAL is depleted by a $50$ MJ strike, it will take several seconds for the SFR to restore that "headroom." If a second strike hits before the coolant has finished cycling, the emitter's available IAL will be lower, resulting in more damage passing through to the armor.

### Maximum Sustained Tank (MST) and Energy Draw

The **Maximum Sustained Tank (MST)** represents the ship's equilibrium point. It is the maximum continuous energy load (in MW) that the shield system can process without depleting the Emitter Thermal Buffer ($IAL$). 

As long as the incoming damage per second (DPS) is less than or equal to the aggregate **SFR** of the active emitters, the $IAL$ remains at 100% capacity. If the incoming load exceeds the MST, the $IAL$ begins to drawdown. Once the $IAL$ is exhausted, any excess energy is passed directly to the armor.

#### Energy Consumption
The power required to sustain the MST is determined by the shield's efficiency:
$$P_{draw} = \frac{Incoming~DPS}{\eta_{shield}}$$
Where $\eta_{shield}$ is the variable efficiency of the hull's specific scale.

*Note: The MST concept is a universal metric for the vessel's thermal rejection architecture and is similarly applied to armor-soak calculations.*

### Tactical Overloading (Redundancy Engagement)

Standard Imperial doctrine typically operates shield arrays at 50% active capacity to maintain the mandated dual-redundancy ($R_{red} = 2$). However, in high-intensity combat, a vessel can perform a **Tactical Overload** by bringing the redundant emitters online simultaneously.

**Effects of Overloading:**
1. **System IAL Boost:** The total energy the shield can absorb in a single strike is doubled, as the load is distributed across the full emitter array.
2. **MST Boost:** The aggregate Sustainable Flow Rate is doubled, allowing the ship to "soak" twice as much continuous damage (MW).

**Risks and Penalties:**
- **Power Strain:** The $P_{draw}$ from the CDAPs doubles to maintain the increased field tension.
- **Thermal Saturation:** Waste heat is dumped into the **Thermal Batteries** at twice the standard rate, rapidly exhausting the ship's defensive endurance.
- **Hardware Fatigue:** Sustained overloading risks "Coolant Cavitation" or manifold warping, potentially lowering the permanent $IAL_{max}$ of the emitters until they are serviced at a USO facility.

*Tactical Application: This is primarily used as a "Shield Flare" to catch high-yield kinetic slugs or torpedoes that would otherwise exceed the standard IAL and penetrate the hull.*

## Armor and Hull Systems

When energy exceeds the shield's IAL or when the shield system is inactive, the vessel's physical structure serves as the primary defense. This is divided into the **Ablative Armor Layer** and the **Structural Hull Frame**.

### The Armor MST (Hardness Rating)

Unlike the shield system, which functions as a drawdown buffer, armor operates as a **Hardness Test**. The **Armor MST** is the fixed MegaJoule (MJ) rating of the plating's structural and thermal resistance.

1. **Soak Threshold:** If the incoming energy (MJ) of a strike is less than or equal to the Armor MST, the armor is effectively unharmed. The plating remains structurally intact, and 100% of the strike's energy is converted into heat and conducted into the ship's **Thermal Batteries**.
2. **Overwhelming the Plate:** If the strike energy exceeds the Armor MST, the armor fails to negate the force. In this event, a fixed percentage ($x\%$) of the damage is absorbed by the armor (causing structural degradation), while the remaining energy passes through to the internal hull frame.

### Unified Structural Integrity

For operational and engineering simplicity, **Armor and Hull are treated as a Unified Structure**. While damage is calculated based on the "Pass-Through" logic described above, both contribute to a single total for the vessel's **Structural Integrity (SI)**.

- **Structural Absorption Rate ($\chi$):** The percentage of overwhelming energy (energy exceeding Armor MST) absorbed by the plating. This rate scales with ship scale ($k$), as larger hulls mount deeper, multi-layered armor lattices that effectively dampen shock and spalling before it reaches the frame.
  - **Small Class (k=1):** $\chi \approx 60\%$ (40% passes to hull).
  - **Titanic Class (k=20):** $\chi \approx 80\%$ (20% passes to hull).
  - **Megastructures (k \ge 160):** $\chi \to 95\%$.
- **Heat Generation:** Even during structural failure, the armor continues to conduct its maximum rated MST as heat into the batteries until the plating is entirely stripped.

*Lore Note: While Structural Integrity (SI) serves as the primary "Hit Point" bucket for gameplay, the **Thermal Batteries** are the true strategic constraint in Leviathan lore. Due to the high efficiency of the Armor MST soak, batteries often reach 100% capacity from converted strike energy long before the physical hull is breached.*

### Field Repair and Structural Breakpoints

Modern Imperial vessels utilize **Service Drones** and **Nanite Coatings** to provide slow but consistent structural healing over time. These automated systems can repair micro-fractures in the armor and stabilize minor hull breaches without crew intervention.

However, this recovery is limited by **Structural Breakpoints** occurring at every **5% increment** of the vessel's total Structural Integrity (SI). These breakpoints serve as a technical handwave for macro-scale structural failure—such as a primary interior beam being bent out of alignment or a reinforced bulkhead fracturing—that is simply too large for small-scale drones or nanite coatings to reconstruct without drydock facilities.

1. **Recovery Limits:** Field repair can only restore Structural Integrity up to the next 5% boundary above the current damage state.
   - *Case A:* Armor that degrades from 100% to 97% SI remains within the primary segment and can recover back to 100%.
   - *Case B:* Armor worn down to 94% SI has crossed the 95% threshold. Service drones can only recover the structure back to the 95% breakpoint.
2. **Maintenance Depth:** This "stair-step" degradation ensures that while a ship can survive multiple skirmishes, sustained combat or a single massive strike will inevitably require drydock reconstruction at a Starbase or USO facility to restore the vessel to its original factory-spec integrity.
3. **Systemic Modularity:** In a game environment, the specific frequency and percentage of these breakpoints may be user-adjustable or affected by ship configuration. Installed modules—such as Reinforced Latticing or Advanced Nanite Hives—can shift these thresholds, allowing a ship to stabilize more damage before a breakpoint is permanently crossed.


### Structural Interaction with Damage Vectors

Physical armor responds differently to the three primary damage vectors compared to energy shields:

*   **Impact Vector vs. Armor:** Armor is the primary defense against kinetic strikes. If the MJ of the impact exceeds the **Armor MST**, the plate suffers a mechanical breach. The "Pass-Through" energy represents spalling and kinetic shock that damages the internal hull frame and sensitive modules.
*   **Radiant Vector vs. Armor:** Armor absorbs radiant energy (lasers/beams) as raw heat. If the incoming MW exceeds the **Armor MST**, the temperature of the Dura-Steel rises. Upon reaching the 1,200 K thermal limit, the armor loses structural integrity and "sloughs off," exposing the hull frame.
*   **Interference Vector vs. Armor:** Because interference weapons target the efficiency of energy bubbles ($\eta_{shield}$), they have negligible effect on physical armor. This makes heavy-armor vessels naturally resistant to "brown-out" tactics.

### Hull Integrity and Breach
The **Structural Hull Frame** is the final layer protecting the crew and internal modules. While armor is designed to be ablative (sacrificial), hull damage represents a compromise of the ship's engineering spine.

Damage to the hull frame results in:
- **Atmospheric Venting:** Loss of cabin pressure and life support.
- **System Malfunction:** Severed wiring harnesses, coolant line ruptures, and sensor misalignment.
- **FTL Decoupling Risk:** Structural damage to the engine mounts can lower the vessel's **C_bind** (Binding Capacity), making high-G maneuvers or FTL spin-up increasingly dangerous (see CX-013).

## Offensive Systems: Damage Vectors

To bridge the gap between naval engineering and tactical doctrine, all offensive weaponry is categorized into three primary **Damage Vectors**. These vectors dictate how incoming energy interacts with the ship's shield and thermal architecture.

### 1. Impact Vector (Kinetic / Explosive)
*   **Primary Target:** $IAL$ (Emitter Thermal Buffer)
*   **Metric:** High MJ / Low $t$ (Duration)
*   **Physics:** These weapons (Railguns, Autocannons, Torpedoes) deliver massive energy packets instantly. Because the impact duration is nearly zero, the ship's $MST$ (coolant cycling) provides negligible benefit. 
*   **Tactical Role:** The "Alpha Strike." Designed to punch through the static IAL buffer and cause immediate "leakage" damage to the hull armor.

### 2. Radiant Vector (Thermal / Directed Energy)
*   **Primary Target:** $MST$ (Maximum Sustained Tank)
*   **Metric:** High MW (Sustained Rate) / High $t$ (Duration)
*   **Physics:** These weapons (Lasers, Phasers, Beams) deliver energy continuously. They pressure the emitter's ability to cycle coolant to the batteries.
*   **Tactical Role:** The "Pressure Cooker." Designed to exceed the ship's $MST$, slowly draining the IAL buffer over time and filling the ship's thermal batteries to force a retreat or a shield collapse.

### 3. Interference Vector (Electromagnetic / Ion)
*   **Primary Target:** $\eta_{shield}$ (Conversion Efficiency)
*   **Metric:** Phase-Matched Energy
*   **Physics:** Interference weapons (Ion Cannons, EM-Pulses) do not rely on raw energy to break a shield. Instead, they introduce "Phase Jitter" into the bubble's tension logic. This effectively lowers the ship's **Shield Efficiency ($\eta_{shield}$)**.
*   **Tactical Role:** The "Efficiency Stripper." A ship under interference fire must draw significantly more power from its CDAPs to sustain the same MST. This is used to "brown out" an enemy vessel, forcing them to choose between maintaining shields or powering their engines and weapons.

## Shield Emitter Specifications

To ensure total hull coverage, shield emitters follow the same Intrinsic Coupling Field (ICF) scaling laws as FTL emitters (see CX-013).

### Scaling and Coverage Math

The effective coverage and thermal capacity of a shield emitter is determined by its power rating ($P_i$):

- **Nominal Bubble Diameter:** $d_i = 2 \times \sqrt{P_i / 1~\text{kW}}~\text{m}$
- **Coverage Patch Area:** $A_{\text{patch},i} = \pi \times P_i$ (where $P_i$ is in kW)

### Absorption Calibration

To ensure mathematical consistency across all combat systems, the **MegaJoule (MJ)** is the standardized unit for "Damage Amounts" (total energy transfer) and "Capacity" (buffer size). The **MegaWatt (MW)** is utilized strictly as a measure of "Rate" (Energy per second).

- **Impact Damage:** Measured in **MJ**.
- **Continuous Damage (DPS):** Measured in **MW** (MJ/s).

Shield performance is calibrated by the emitter's power rating ($P_i$):

1. **Instantaneous Absorption Limit ($IAL_{max}$):** The maximum "Static" MJ buffer per emitter (Localized Hit Points).
   $$IAL_{max} = P_i \times \kappa_{burst}$$
   *Baseline $\kappa_{burst} = 0.5$ MJ/kW*

2. **Sustainable Flow Rate ($SFR_i$):** The regeneration rate of an individual emitter's buffer.
   $$SFR_i = P_i \times \kappa_{flow}$$
   *Baseline $\kappa_{flow} = 10.0$ (Unitless ratio)*

3. **Maximum Sustained Tank ($MST$):** The vessel's total continuous MW "Soak" limit (Standard Active Array).
   $$MST = \sum (SFR_i)$$

4. **Shield Efficiency ($\eta_{shield}$):** The conversion ratio based on the ship's linear scale factor ($k$) relative to the Falcon.
   $$\eta_{shield} = \eta_{base} \times k^{-0.4}$$
   *Baseline $\eta_{base} = 3.0$*

### Redundancy Doctrine

Standard Imperial naval architecture mandates a **dual-redundant** ($R_{\text{red}} = 2$) shield configuration. The number of emitters required is calculated by:

$$N_{\text{emit}} = \lceil (2 \times A_{\text{box}}) / A_{\text{patch},i} \rceil$$

Where $A_{\text{box}}$ is the estimated surface area of the ship's service envelope.

### Emitter Class Bins

Shield emitters are classified into standardized power bins. Larger hulls typically utilize larger emitter classes to reduce total part count and maintenance overhead.

| Emitter Bin | Single-Emitter Rating ($P_i$) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Tiny** | $P_i < 1~\text{kW}$ |
| **Small** | $1 \le P_i < 100~\text{kW}$ |
| **Medium** | $100 \le P_i < 1,000~\text{kW}$ |
| **Large** | $1,000 \le P_i < 10,000~\text{kW}$ |
| **Huge** | $10,000 \le P_i < 20,000~\text{kW}$ |
| **Capital** | $20,000 \le P_i < 100,000~\text{kW}$ |
| **Titanic** | $P_i \ge 100,000~\text{kW}$ |

*Note: While shield emitters are standard hull components, they are often mixed in size—using large emitters for broad hull faces and smaller units for contour closure and redundancy pockets.*
