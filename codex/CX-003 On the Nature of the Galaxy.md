# CX-003: On the Nature of the Galaxy

Document Status: Draft
Primary Layer: Multi-Layer
Subject Domain: Civilization
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This volume establishes the broad civilizational condition of the Leviathan setting. It outlines the uncertain Terran origin of humanity, the lost age of intergalactic expansion, the legendary Great War of the Capitals, the long recovery that followed, the rise of the Imperial Family after the War of Chaos, and the modern imperial order built around houses, Wardens, the Union for Space Operations, the Imperial Survey Corps, and megacorporations. Its central claim is that the galaxy is not dead and not safe: it is ordered, tense, and haunted by gaps in the historical record.

## Diegetic Record

The galaxy of the modern age does not arrive to its inhabitants as a continuous inheritance. It comes in layers: the hint of a pre-human presence, the ruins of forgotten human expansions, the scars of ancient wars large enough to break planets and depopulate systems, and a present empire only recently secure enough to call itself the guardian of civilization.

The oldest human horizon recoverable to modern thought is the Terran Era, when mankind is believed to have lived in the Milky Way. Little is known with confidence. What followed was a lost age of expansion in which humanity spread beyond a single galaxy. At some later point, war erupted on a scale now difficult to credit. No surviving authority can say how many galaxies humanity had colonized, only that it was more than one.

From this darkness emerges the greatest of the old war-legends: the Great War of the Capitals. Twelve colossal capital ships from twelve rival powers are said to have fought an automated war for thousands of years with cloned crews, shadowed at all times by a neutral thirteenth vessel. Tradition claims the ships were so immense that when two passed one another the transit took a full day, an event remembered as a Judgment Day. Entire sections of the vessels remained sealed for generations, and crews sometimes spent years merely exploring the architecture they served. When a capital ship was destroyed, its crew was said to be resurrected aboard the thirteenth ship and held there until another hull of the proper designation could be found. Some of these replacement vessels appeared pristine. Others seemed to have rested in storage for tens of thousands of years, with internal ecosystems growing in forgotten chambers. Whatever the precise truth, the story matters because the survivors of that war are remembered as the seed-stock of the original twelve cultures.

After the war came the Great Silence, a long age of recovery during which those cultures rebuilt in partial isolation. The duration is unclear, though modern estimates place it at no less than many tens of thousands of years. Later came the era of the Five Republics, which in turn ended in the War of Chaos. That final conflict brought forth the Imperial Family and began Imperial Year 0.

The empire that followed did not abolish conflict. It formalized it. Twelve noble houses, later expanded to twenty-four, were appointed to manage the galaxy under imperial sovereignty. A later rebellion further hardened the settlement. Thirty-one native tribes who defended the Imperial Palace were granted special charter as Wardens: territoryless military lineages empowered to command ships under contract. At the same time the Union for Space Operations forced negotiation by holding the docking network hostage. The resulting order divided power three ways. Houses maintained fleets. The Union crewed ships and bound void labor into one common body. Wardens alone held command authority over those fleets. The empire accepted this tension because it judged managed rivalry safer than unchecked concentration of force.

In the present age, the Imperial Survey Corps periodically defines new territories for campaigns of rediscovery. It surveys large regions of space, identifies roughly five to seven thousand economically viable star systems, lays initial stargate infrastructure, and assigns houses to prepare those territories for settlement. A campaign formally ends when the local Imperial Eye, a special inter-territory gate, is completed. Campaign 1, Territory 0 is the Throne Worlds themselves, where the great houses, clans, and megacorporations reside.

Thus the modern galaxy is best understood as an uneasy enlightenment. The empire and imperial family are broadly benevolent, but also cynical. They believe conflict is ineradicable and that too much peace breeds fragility. They maintain order while deliberately preserving structural tension between houses, Wardens, unions, and corporations. This is why the galaxy feels post-grimdark rather than utopian: civilization has survived the worst, but no one fully trusts the past, the present, or the silence waiting in unlit places. Lost colonies are found empty. Some seem abandoned for millennia. Others look as if they were vacated moments ago, with hot food on a table and a cigarette still burning. What unsettles the setting is not only what remains, but what is missing.

## Analytical Breakdown

### Layer Function

- Eldritch Layer: Gives the galaxy its oldest uncertainty. It suggests that something was present before humanity and that absence itself can be evidence.
- Hyborean Era: Carries the fragmentary inheritance of Terran origin, lost expansion, and the earliest legendary human dispersals.
- Ancient History: Holds the great scars of civilizational violence, including dead fleets, shattered worlds, and the long shadow of wars too large for later societies to fully comprehend.
- Modern Times: Begins in earnest with Imperial Year 0 and contains the formal institutions now attempting to discipline rediscovery, sovereignty, labor, warfare, and memory.

### Origins

The setting begins with a human origin point that is known in outline but not in trustworthy detail. The Terran Era establishes mankind's ancestral home in the Milky Way, but almost nothing from that age survives in a fully reliable form. The Lost Age of Expansion follows, marking the moment when humanity ceased to be a one-galaxy civilization. This origin structure matters because it denies the present a complete founding archive. The empire inherits humanity's legacy, but not its full explanation.

### Development

The key developmental sequence of the galaxy is as follows:

- Terran Era: Humanity lives in the Milky Way.
- Lost Age of Expansion: Humanity spreads through more than one galaxy.
- Intergalactic War: A vast conflict breaks out across humanity's wider sphere.
- Great War of the Capitals: Twelve capital ships and a mysterious thirteenth vessel define the mythic military inheritance of the setting.
- Great Silence: The surviving cultures rebuild over an immense span of time.
- Era of the Five Republics: Regional civilizational order returns in multipolar form.
- War of Chaos: The republic era collapses into decisive conflict.
- Imperial Year 0: The Imperial Family rises and founds the present order.

### Present Understanding

The current galaxy is organized around several mutually constraining power centers.

- Imperial Family and Houses: The empire governs through the Imperial Family, twenty-three major houses, and a wider body of lesser houses. The original twelve-house settlement remains culturally formative, but the mature imperial order distributes authority through a broader feudal hierarchy.
- Wardens: The thirty-one chartered clans provide command authority while holding no territory of their own, making them militarily essential but politically dependent.
- Union for Space Operations: All people operating beyond a planet's immediate territory fall within the union's labor order, and ships are crewed through this structure even when their owners are political rivals.
- Imperial Survey Corps: The ISC converts rediscovery into a repeatable process by defining territories, opening campaigns, and laying the first gate infrastructure needed for settlement.
- Megacorporations: Corporate power rivals that of the houses through control of encrypted automated manufacturing systems and the industrial leverage that follows.

The result is a civilization that works by controlled friction. The empire is not simply centralized, federal, or feudal. It is a managed balance of throne, house, union, clan, and corporation.

## Material Implications

- Historical tone: The audience should feel uncertainty about whether the inherited account of the past is complete, accurate, or politically curated.
- Political structure: The tripartite military settlement prevents any one institution from fully monopolizing force, but guarantees continuing strategic tension.
- Economic structure: Megacorporate control over encrypted automated manufacturing turns industry into a second power base alongside aristocratic governance.
- Military structure: Fleets are owned, crewed, and commanded by different institutions, making war logistically possible but politically constrained.
- Exploration model: ISC campaigns provide a controlled framework for rediscovering, opening, and contesting new territories at scale.
- Atmospheric identity: The setting is post-grimdark because order exists and is worth preserving, yet the universe still feels strained, haunted, and potentially unstable.

## Author Notes

Purpose: This volume establishes the broad historical and institutional baseline of the setting before more focused volumes define factions, territories, religions, wars, and technologies.

Reasoning: The galaxy needs a managed frame that explains why the empire exists, why rediscovery is ongoing, why the past is unreliable, and why the modern order remains tense even while it is broadly benevolent. The post-grimdark stance is being used here to create uncertainty in the audience about the validity, completeness, and interpretation of the past rather than to imply that civilization has already failed.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Eldritch Layer: Introduces unease through the suggestion of an older, non-human prior condition that can be felt more easily than explained.
- Hyborean Era: Organizes the legendary and partially recoverable human deep past, including expansion myths and inherited civilizational fragments.
- Ancient History: Organizes the visible scars of major wars, lost states, ghost fleets, and shattered worlds whose consequences are material even when their record is incomplete.
- Modern Times: Organizes the imperial attempt to formalize history, survey space, govern rediscovery, and hold civilization together after the War of Chaos.

Math And Constraint Checks: If capital ships are large enough to take a day to pass one another, later volumes need concrete size, maintenance, crew, automation, and production assumptions that justify such vessels as exceptional relics rather than normal warships. If each ISC campaign opens five to seven thousand viable systems, later volumes need realistic assumptions about survey capacity, gate-construction pace, colonization throughput, and imperial oversight. If fleets are split among house ownership, union labor, and Warden command, later volumes need clear rules for doctrine, latency, contract limits, and wartime failure modes.

FTL Hand-Waves: Stargates and inter-territory gates are now grounded at a high level by CX-013's CHJ model, but this volume still does not attempt to define their exact timings, engineering detail, or exceptional edge cases. The thirteenth ship's resurrection or crew-restoration function is treated here as legendary or unresolved rather than as explained modern canon technology.

Open Questions: How much of the Great War of the Capitals is literal history and how much is mythic memory; what was the thirteenth ship; why are some rediscovered colonies empty in impossible ways; how exactly did the Five Republics function; what legal and technical limits maintain corporate control of manufacturing; what operational limits constrain Warden command and ISC campaign cadence in practice.
