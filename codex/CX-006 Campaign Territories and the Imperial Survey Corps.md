# CX-006: Campaign Territories and the Imperial Survey Corps

Document Status: Draft
Primary Layer: Modern Times
Subject Domain: Civilization
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This volume defines the empire's recurring campaigns of rediscovery. It explains how the Imperial Survey Corps opens new territories, how those territories are reduced into regions, survey sectors, and systems, how Houses, Wardens, and corporations are forced to bootstrap frontier economies under constrained infrastructure, how the ISC's public role as benevolent explorer coexists with classification and selective silence, how territorial outcomes are fixed when the Imperial Eye is completed, and why concluded campaigns remain permanent scars in Imperial archives.

## Diegetic Record

The modern empire does not expand as a smooth advancing frontier. It expands by campaign. The galaxy is too broken, too unevenly mapped, and too full of historical residue for simple annexation. Instead, the empire repeatedly opens bounded territories, subjects them to disciplined survey, and forces its subordinate powers to prove that they can turn contested space into governed space.

This work falls first to the Imperial Survey Corps. The ISC identifies a territory, determines the cluster of economically viable systems within it, reduces that space into a usable survey lattice, and lays the initial gate and survey infrastructure needed to make entry possible. That first access is not intended to be comfortable. It is constrained by design. Houses, Wardens, and corporate actors are expected to build under pressure, using local resources and incomplete knowledge while the permanent arteries of integration are still absent.

Publicly, this makes the ISC look almost philanthropic: the institution that maps the dark, names the unknown, rescues the lost, and makes new settlement possible. That reputation is not wholly false. But the Corps also carries a quieter mandate. It classifies dangerous findings, withholds records it judges destabilizing, and maintains sealed archives whose existence is easier to infer than to prove. The same organization that opens the frontier also decides which portions of it are safe to become common knowledge.

A territory is not the same thing as a constitutional sector. In imperial law and house politics, a sector may mean a broad enduring district of rule. In ISC practice, a survey sector is a second-order cell inside a campaign territory, nested under a region and above individual systems. The operative survey stack is territory to region to sector to system. Arrays, governorships, service contracts, and settlement claims are adjudicated against that lattice because survey, routing, and archives are all built on it. In frontier settlement practice, the survey sector is also the normal scale of fiefdom grant. Houses receive stewardship over sectors, then appoint governors, charter holders, or subordinate clients across the individual systems inside them.

Heavy gates bind regions to one another. Medium gates bind sectors within a region. Light gates bind local systems within a sector. The frontier is therefore not merely politically staged; it is physically organized into layers of movement and authority before any permanent courtly names or honors are attached to it.

The empire uses this structure to keep the frontier politically alive. Houses compete for standing and future fiefdom. Wardens compete for contract, prestige, and leverage. Corporations compete for industrial foothold. The ISC, outwardly a survey institution, functions in practice as the administrative midwife of new imperial space.

This dual role creates enduring tension. Houses suspect the Corps of burying profitable or politically decisive discoveries behind safety doctrine. Scholars accuse it of maiming the archive in the name of order. Cults and contrarian orders are fed by every silence they cannot penetrate. Even loyal survey officers know that some findings vanish into restricted channels, some sponsoring powers are informed only privately, and some lines of inquiry end in revoked credentials rather than publication. The ISC justifies this as containment. Much of the empire experiences it as paternal secrecy backed by Imperial force.

The campaign ends when the Imperial Eye, the permanent inter-territory gate of the region, is completed. At that point the territory ceases to be a frontier experiment and becomes part of the settled imperial body. Its names, contracts, constructions, grudges, and archival claims do not disappear when the frontier closes. They become political memory.

## Analytical Breakdown

### Layer Function

- Modern Times: This is a primary modern-layer structure. It explains how the empire presently rediscovers, integrates, and contests space.
- Ancient History: Campaigns constantly encounter ancient debris, lost states, and incomplete records, forcing the frontier to operate on top of older scars.
- Hyborean Era: Rediscovered territories often contain legendary remnants and material inheritances whose meaning remains contested.
- Eldritch Layer: Frontier survey is one of the principal ways in which modern institutions stumble into pre-human or ontologically hostile residue.

### Origins

Campaign structure emerges from the fact that the empire is rediscovering rather than merely conquering the galaxy. Large portions of space are only partially known, unevenly inhabited, or politically unresolved. The ISC model turns that uncertainty into a repeatable imperial process.

### Development

The campaign system matured into several standard features.

- The ISC surveys and classifies a territory before opening it.
- The ISC resolves that territory into first-order regions and second-order survey sectors before full political settlement occurs.
- The ISC maintains sealed archival and classification channels for findings judged contaminating, destabilizing, or politically explosive.
- Initial gate access is provided in constrained form to force local development rather than effortless extraction.
- Houses compete to establish arrays, secure sector stewardships, and assign governorships within those awarded sectors.
- High-evidence discoveries may be sequestered from public archives, contested by their commissioning powers, or shifted into restricted notice rather than open publication.
- Permanent integration occurs only after completion of the Imperial Eye and the associated settlement threshold.
- Contractual work performed during the campaign may continue to matter after sovereignty changes through archival claims, standing, and long-tail compensation structures.

### Territorial Stack

- Territory: The bounded campaign volume opened for rediscovery, contest, and eventual settlement.
- Region: The first-order survey cell inside a territory. Heavy-gate routing and high-level territorial structure operate at this scale.
- Survey Sector: The second-order survey cell inside a region. Medium-gate anchors, local industrial clustering, and navigational handoff operate at this scale.
- System: The individual addressed star system inside a survey sector. Local light-gate traffic operates at this scale.

### Present Understanding

The current campaign order includes the following working assumptions.

- A typical territory contains roughly five to seven thousand economically viable star systems; the canonical Throne pass T0 currently uses seven thousand.
- The current standard survey frame resolves a territory into sixteen primary regions.
- Each region subdivides into between four and nine survey sectors according to viable volume and sample density.
- Heavy gates bridge regions, medium gates bridge sectors within a region, and light gates bind local systems inside a sector.
- Survey sectors rather than individual systems are the standard frontier fiefdom unit; system governance is usually delegated beneath that sector-scale grant.
- Canonical addresses preserve the full stack, for example `T0R07S03-SYS12`, so archives survive political renaming.
- Publicly, the ISC is celebrated as the empire's great exploratory service; privately, it also functions as a classification and containment bureaucracy.
- Discoveries that imply memetic hazard, archival mutilation, pre-human occupation, or large-scale historical instability may be sealed, sequestered, or denied formal recognition in public channels.
- This secrecy produces a permanent adversarial fringe of defectors, leak networks, rival scholars, and Houses willing to gamble political risk for suppressed knowledge.
- Campaigns are arenas of managed scarcity, not simply colonization drives.
- Territorial naming, construction records, and service claims persist in Imperial archives even after political control changes.
- Campaign closure triggers an Imperial settlement event that liquidates or conditionally carries forward campaign-scoped holdings under continuity rules set by the Throne.
- Success may produce durable fiefdom, prestige, and future leverage; failure may produce redistribution, humiliation, and inherited resentment.

## Material Implications

- The frontier remains a permanent engine of political competition rather than a one-time historical episode.
- The ISC becomes one of the empire's most strategically important institutions because it determines where and how new struggle begins.
- The survey lattice lets the empire keep freight, law, and military movement speaking the same spatial language.
- Sector-scale grants give Houses enough depth to matter strategically without collapsing campaign politics into one-system micro-fiefdoms.
- The ISC's authority over classification lets it mediate not just access to space, but access to truth about that space.
- Political and religious fringe movements become more believable because the Corps' silences create constituencies that think ignorance itself is the greater danger.
- Early scarcity justifies smuggling, sabotage, industrial improvisation, and desperate alliances.
- Territorial persistence means that even a concluded campaign continues to shape income, honor, rivalry, and narrative identity long afterward.
- Stable survey designators let courtly renaming, house claims, and later civic identities accumulate without destroying archive continuity.
- The campaign model provides a diegetic framework for cyclical conflict without implying that the empire is unserious about settlement.

## Author Notes

Purpose: This volume turns the broad claim of imperial rediscovery in CX-003 into a concrete organizational framework.

Reasoning: The setting needs a disciplined explanation for why the empire repeatedly opens new contested spaces and why those spaces matter after the moment of conquest. Campaign territories solve both the lore problem and the downstream design problem. The GalaxySim territory generator also makes the internal survey grammar more precise than this volume previously did, especially around the distinction between constitutional sectors and survey sectors. This pass also reincorporates legacy ISC lore that treated the Corps not only as survey authority but as a secrecy-bearing archive and containment institution.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Modern Times: Defines the empire's current mechanism for expansion and reintegration.
- Ancient History: Ensures every frontier can also be an archaeological and political wound.
- Hyborean Era: Lets campaigns uncover mythic remnants without forcing them into complete explanation.
- Eldritch Layer: Keeps the frontier as a contact zone with older and more dangerous layers of reality.

Math And Constraint Checks: If each campaign contains five to seven thousand viable systems, later volumes need realistic assumptions about survey rate, infrastructure crews, administrative burden, and settlement throughput. If the current standard survey frame uses sixteen regions with four to nine survey sectors each, later volumes need freight, fleet doctrine, and administrative staffing assumptions that respect that internal hierarchy. If the ISC can meaningfully classify, suppress, and compartmentalize frontier findings at campaign scale, later volumes need plausible staffing, vault infrastructure, legal authority, and leak pressure sufficient to make that secrecy credible rather than magical. If campaigns end only when the Imperial Eye is completed, later volumes need credible timelines and bottlenecks for gate construction. If archival claims continue to matter after sovereignty change, later volumes need enforceable ledger and legal systems to keep that memory economically real.

FTL Hand-Waves: This volume accepts CX-013's CHJ baseline for hyperspace, jump space, and gate classes, but it still leaves exact frontier transit timings, Imperial Eye class identity, and detailed gate engineering to the continuing FTL rewrite.

Open Questions: What is the standard duration of a campaign in-universe; how are viable systems ranked by the ISC; what threshold converts a survey sector or array into durable fiefdom; what formal classes of discovery trigger mandatory suppression or private notice; what categories of archival claim survive sovereignty transfer; how many campaigns may run concurrently in different constitutional sectors or territories.
