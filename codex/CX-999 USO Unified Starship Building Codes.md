# USO Unified Starship Building Codes

## Chapter 1. General Provisions and ICS-SCU Layer

### 101 Purpose

The USO Unified Starship Building Codes establish the minimum recognized construction standard for habitable starship structures operating under Imperial law, USO certification, or House and corporate contract.

This code shall be read and applied in construction order, progressing from primary structural standards through habitat integration, service interconnects, and operational readiness requirements.

### 102 Standard Corridor Unit (SCU)

#### 102.0 Terminology

For the purposes of this code, the term habitat shall mean an internal structured space within a starship.

SCUs linked together in series shall be recognized as forming the spine of the ship.

#### 102.0A Interior Conditioned Space (ICS)

The completed SCU spine, including all connected conditioned SCU habitats, shall be designated the Interior Conditioned Space (ICS) of the vessel.

Any airlock constructed and certified as an SCU habitat shall be included in the vessel ICS for classification, maintenance, and survivability compliance.

#### 102.0B ICS Core Facility Minimums

Each vessel shall provide, within its certified ICS, not fewer than the following core facilities:

1. One primary engine bay.
2. One power-generation facility.
3. Two environmental control facilities.
4. One thermal management center.
5. One command center, designated as a bridge or cockpit according to ship class.

#### 102.0C Small-Class Consolidation Allowance

On small-class vessels where full separation is impracticable, two or more core facility functions may be consolidated into shared spaces or integrated assemblies, provided all required functions remain present and certifiable.

#### 102.0D Ship-Level Emergency Endurance Requirement

Regardless of ship size or consolidation strategy, every vessel shall sustain its certified crew complement for not less than 90 days during emergency isolation conditions.

This requirement applies equally to single-crew craft, including fighters, bombers, and comparable small-hull vessels.

Required endurance provisioning shall include waste-management capability sized for the certified crew complement and endurance duration. Minimum survivability provisioning shall be used as a controlling factor in lower-bound ship-size determination.

#### 102.0E Single-Occupant Survivability Doctrine

For single-occupant craft, emergency endurance compliance may be satisfied by a combined survivability package of onboard systems and externally stowed survival stores, provided the total package supports the certified occupant for the required endurance duration.

#### 102.0F External Survival Store Accessibility

Where survival stores are stowed outside immediate cockpit reach, access shall be permitted through short-duration EVA operations using the vessel's certified environmental suit doctrine.

External survival stores shall be mounted for controlled retrieval after emergency landing, hard docking, or stationary anchoring and shall not require specialized support equipment unavailable to the certified occupant.

#### 102.0G Planetary and Non-Habitable Surface Survival Intent

Single-occupant survivability packages shall support emergency shelter-in-place on habitable planetary surfaces and temporary camp establishment on non-habitable bodies, including asteroids, until rescue or recovery operations are completed.

#### 102.0H Emergency Beacon Requirement

All single-occupant craft shall include a distress-beacon capability suitable for unattended operation during pilot incapacitation or off-craft shelter operations.

#### 102.0I Minimum Viable Single-Occupant Envelope (MVSOE)

For single-occupant craft certified under Sections 102.0E through 102.0H, the minimum viable ship envelope shall be evaluated as:

MVSOE = V_hab_core + V_life_support_core + V_waste_core + V_power_core + V_eva_interface + V_external_store_mounts + V_structure_margin

Where:

1. V_hab_core is the conditioned occupant command and rest volume.
2. V_life_support_core is atmosphere management and water-recovery volume required for certified endurance.
3. V_waste_core is waste capture, stabilization, and transfer volume for certified endurance.
4. V_power_core is sustained emergency power generation or storage volume for certified endurance.
5. V_eva_interface is suit access, hatch, and retrieval interface volume required to recover external survival stores.
6. V_external_store_mounts is the integrated mount and release envelope for externally stowed certified survival stores.
7. V_structure_margin is the minimum structural and integration allowance required by hull-class certification.

A vessel is non-compliant where demonstrated available envelope is less than MVSOE for the declared mission and endurance profile.

#### 102.0J Compile Validation Requirement for MVSOE

Each compile pass for a single-occupant craft shall calculate MVSOE from current design data, compare it to available certified envelope, and issue an explicit pass or fail determination.

Compile output shall include, at minimum, the current value of each MVSOE term, the net envelope margin, identified deficits, and the corrective actions required to restore compliance.

#### 102.0K Certification Gate

No single-occupant design may advance to the next construction-order stage unless the most recent compile record demonstrates MVSOE compliance and is countersigned by a certified engineering authority.

The terms habitat and module shall not be treated as interchangeable. Habitat refers to internal structured space. Module terminology shall be reserved for ship systems, replaceable assemblies, or other classifications defined elsewhere in this code.

#### 102.1 Definition

The Standard Corridor Unit (SCU) is the base habitable structural module recognized by the USO for starship planning, certification, and interior volumetric measurement.

#### 102.2 Dimensional Standard

One SCU shall consist of a self-contained structure measuring 6 meters by 6 meters by 18 meters.

The dimensions assigned to an SCU are its exterior dimensions, or outside dimensions (OD).

#### 102.3 Habitat Rating

An SCU shall be rated to sustain 8 human occupants for a continuous duration of 90 days without external resupply, provided the unit remains structurally intact and its life-support envelope is operational.

#### 102.4 Functional Intent

An SCU shall be treated as a survivable habitat unit rather than as a disposable escape system. The code basis for the SCU assumes that deep-space abandonment may place survivors multiple light-years from recoverable infrastructure, making short-duration escape pods inadequate as a primary life-preservation strategy.

#### 102.5 Door Openings

An SCU shall normally provide a pair of end doors, with one door assembly located at each short-end face.

An SCU may provide not more than 2 side doors on each long-edge face.

#### 102.5A Interconnect Buffer Envelope

Each SCU shall include a predefined 1 meter interconnect buffer envelope around the full exterior of the unit.

#### 102.5B End-to-End Coupling Geometry

Where two SCUs are installed end to end, each unit shall retain its own end sheer door at the shared interface, and a nominal 1 meter gap shall be maintained between the opposing door planes as the interconnect zone.

#### 102.5C Interconnect Zone Function

The interconnect buffer and interface zones shall be treated as controlled service space used for cable runs, pipe routing, conduit access, door clearance, seal transition, and interface maintenance, and shall not be assigned as primary habitat occupancy area.

#### 102.5C.1 Mechanical Tolerances at SCU Coupling Interfaces

All SCU-to-SCU coupling interfaces shall accommodate the following mechanical tolerances to prevent structural failure under dynamic maneuvers, thermal cycling, and load asymmetries:

**Lateral deflection (perpendicular to spine axis):** 15 to 20 millimeters per coupling interface, allowing side-to-side flex without binding or shear-door misalignment.

**Vertical deflection (perpendicular to spine axis, orthogonal to lateral):** 15 to 20 millimeters per coupling interface, accommodating thermal expansion and uneven gravitational loading during acceleration events.

**Angular tolerance at coupling (pitch and roll):** 0.5 to 1.0 degree per coupling interface, permitting torsional compliance under asymmetric thrust or unbalanced load distribution along the spine.

**Axial tolerance (compression and extension along spine axis):** 5 to 10 millimeters per coupling interface, absorbing thermal cycling and structural settlement while maintaining sheer-door alignment and EIP service continuity.

#### 102.5D Environmental Interconnect Ports (EIP) Requirement

#### 102.5D Environmental Interconnect Ports (EIP) Requirement

All SCUs shall implement standard USO Environmental Interconnect Ports (EIP) for connection to ship-provided services.

#### 102.5E Required Service Domains

The EIP interface shall support, at minimum, atmospheric supply, water supply and recovery, waste transfer and processing interface, thermal exchange, and electrical power transfer as required by the assigned habitat class and endurance rating.

#### 102.5E.1 Standard Port Bank Count

The EIP shall be implemented as a standardized bank of 26 ports.

#### 102.5E.2 Standard Port Schedule

The 26-port EIP bank shall be allocated as follows:

1. Potable water supply A  
2. Potable water supply B  
3. Water reclamation return A  
4. Water reclamation return B  
5. Greywater return A  
6. Greywater return B  
7. Blackwater waste transfer A  
8. Blackwater waste transfer B  
9. Thermal management fluid supply A  
10. Thermal management fluid supply B  
11. Thermal management fluid return A  
12. Thermal management fluid return B  
13. Breathing atmosphere supply A  
14. Breathing atmosphere supply B  
15. Atmosphere return and scrubbing feed A  
16. Atmosphere return and scrubbing feed B  
17. Pressure equalization and rapid repress line A  
18. Pressure equalization and rapid repress line B  
19. Fire suppression agent feed A  
20. Fire suppression agent feed B  
21. AC power hot line A  
22. AC power hot line B  
23. AC power neutral line A  
24. AC power neutral line B  
25. Protective ground A  
26. Protective ground B

#### 102.5E.3 Redundancy Doctrine

Where a service domain is designated A/B in the standard port schedule, the paired lines shall be treated as independent redundant paths. Failure, contamination, or isolation of one path shall not, by itself, remove that domain from SCU service.

#### 102.5E.4 Port Reassignment Limits

Manufacturers may not reassign, omit, or repurpose any of the 26 standard ports in a way that breaks USO interchange compatibility. Additional proprietary service ports may be provided only as supplemental interfaces outside the mandatory 26-port bank.

#### 102.5E.5 EIP Manifold Block Dimensions

The standard EIP manifold assembly shall be dimensioned at 1.0 meter (width) × 0.4 meter (height) × 0.35 meter (depth).

#### 102.5E.6 Manifold Consumption

The EIP manifold shall be considered part of the SCU or habitat trim allowance and counts toward the declared trim consumption budget.

#### 102.5E.7 Per-Habitat Manifold Requirement

Every habitat shall include at least one standard EIP manifold assembly.

#### 102.5E.8 Multi-Manifold Habitats

Large consolidated habitats or technically demanding spaces (e.g., engineering bays managing primary life support) may require two or more EIP manifold assemblies to ensure adequate service distribution and redundancy.

#### 102.5E.9 Manifold Specification and Retrofit

The number of required manifolds, their mounting locations, and their integration into the habitat design shall be specified by the manufacturer or integrator and submitted as part of habitat certification documentation.

#### 102.5F Informal Alias: Shore Power Port (SPP)

The informal term Shore Power Port (SPP) is recognized as an accepted alias for EIP in commercial and consumer-facing materials.

#### 102.5G Usage Convention

Engineering specifications, certification filings, and maintenance records should use the term EIP as the primary designation. Marketing literature and sales catalogs may use SPP or EIP, provided both terms are treated as equivalent interface references.

#### 102.5H Manufacturer Variant Interfaces

Manufacturers may develop proprietary or branded EIP port assemblies, connector housings, or coupling schemes.

#### 102.5I Compliance Constraint

Any manufacturer variant EIP implementation shall remain fully compliant with USO interface standards for safety, interoperability, and service continuity at ship-integration boundaries.

#### 102.5J Airlock Conduct Prohibition

USO standards do not permit certified airlocks to be used for punitive depressurization or intentional expulsion of persons to vacuum.

#### 102.5K Airlock Ballistic Rating Requirement

All certified airlocks shall be rated for small-arms firing containment under standard interior pressure conditions.

#### 102.5L Automated Airlock Lifeform Detection Interlock

All certified airlock control systems shall incorporate a continuous lifeform detection function operating throughout any active cycle. Where a detectable lifeform signature is present within the airlock volume, the control system shall immediately suspend and shall not execute any depressurization sequence, exterior vent, or emergency jettison command until the volume is confirmed clear.

This interlock shall not be defeatable by standard crew interface. Override authority is limited to designated command-level personnel acting under documented emergency conditions, and all such overrides shall generate a permanent log entry timestamped to the ship's master chronometer.

#### 102.6 Orientation and Gravity Constraint

SCUs shall be treated as equatorially bound modules under artificial gravity conditions. Floor and ceiling penetrations are not part of the normal certified door arrangement.

#### 102.7 Maximum Door Count

The theoretical maximum number of door-capable faces in a fully penetrated SCU is 10. Under normal certification, an SCU shall not exceed 6 doors.

#### 102.8 Measurement Use

The SCU shall serve as a standard unit of measure for starship interior planning, compartment scheduling, and habitable fit-out descriptions.

#### 102.9 Measurement Expression

Where a compartment or fitted interior is described in SCU notation, the sequence shall be expressed as width x length x height in whole SCU counts unless otherwise noted.

Example: A sick bay designated as a 2 x 3 x 2 SCU interior habitat occupies the equivalent fitted volume of 12 SCUs.

#### 102.10 Interior Clear Volume

The interior dimensions of an SCU shall be understood as trim-line dimensions derived from the SCU exterior shell and adjusted for ship purpose, fit-out standard, and internal systems integration.

#### 102.11 Standard Trim Allowance

Unless a more specific occupancy, mission, or hull-purpose standard is provided elsewhere in this code, interior trim and fitted systems shall be assumed to consume approximately 25 percent of the internal gross volume of an SCU.

#### 102.12 Trim-Line Components

Trim allowance may include paneling, integral storage, service routing, environmental housings, concealed sanitary fixtures, bunks, fold-down furnishings, and other built-in equipment necessary to make the SCU habitable for its rated use.

#### 102.13 Purpose-Based Variation

The usable interior volume and trim-line arrangement of an SCU may vary by ship purpose and compartment function, provided the module remains compliant with the applicable occupancy, endurance, and survivability rating.

#### 102.14 Consolidated Habitats and Bays

Multiple SCUs may be manufactured, sold, and installed as a consolidated habitat, bay, ward, barracks, or service volume, provided the combined assembly remains dimensionally aligned to the SCU exterior grid.

Examples of consolidated SCU assemblies include cargo bays, medical bays, barracks, workshops, and other purpose-built interior volumes.

Large technical habitats may also be expressed in consolidated SCU form. An engine room, for example, may be designated as a 5 x 5 x 3 SCU habitat.

#### 102.15 Consolidated SCU Notation

Consolidated SCU assemblies shall be expressed in SCU counts using the same width x length x height notation used for single-module planning.

Example: A cargo bay designated as 3 x 3 x 3 is a consolidated assembly occupying 27 SCUs by exterior dimensional standard.

Example: An engine room designated as 5 x 5 x 3 is a consolidated assembly occupying 75 SCUs by exterior dimensional standard.

#### 102.16 Interior Reclamation in Consolidated Assemblies

Where adjacent SCUs are combined into a single functional interior, intervening trim zones, non-structural partitions, duplicated fittings, and other redundant internal elements may be reduced or eliminated to reclaim additional usable interior space.

#### 102.17 Exterior Grid Continuity

Interior reclamation in a consolidated assembly shall not alter the exterior dimensional basis of the SCU standard. The outside dimensions of the assembly shall remain measurable by whole SCU increments for manufacture, transport, certification, and ship integration.

#### 102.18 Manufactured Standardization

Cargo bays, medical bays, barracks, and similar consolidated habitat products shall be considered standard manufactured SCU-based assemblies when designed for sale, fabrication, or certification under USO-recognized shipbuilding practice.

#### 102.19 Manufacturer Trim Disclosure

Any manufacturer, shipyard, or certified integrator offering an SCU habitat product for sale, contract bid, or certification review shall disclose the trim consumption percentage associated with each offered trim line and habitat configuration.

#### 102.20 Configuration-Specific Reporting

Trim consumption disclosure shall be specific to the declared SCU configuration and shall not be represented as a universal value across all sizes, classes, or fit-out variants unless separately verified for each variant.

#### 102.21 Published Interior Efficiency Claims

Where an advertisement, catalog entry, or procurement listing includes an interior efficiency statement, the listing shall state the trim line designation and the corresponding trim consumption value used to derive that statement.

#### 102.22 Minimum Disclosure Content

A compliant trim disclosure shall include, at minimum, the habitat class, SCU configuration, trim line name, trim consumption percentage, and the resulting estimated usable interior percentage.

#### 102.23 Example Disclosure Statement

Example: The Meherger Corp MBV-2A Medical Bay is available in 3 x 3 x 2 configuration. With the Platinum trim line, it provides an efficient 15 percent trim consumption without compromising quality.

#### 102.24 Compliance and Certification Use

Trim disclosure records shall be made available for USO certification review and shall be retained in procurement and ship-fit documentation so interior-space claims can be audited during build acceptance, refit approval, and operational recertification.

#### 102.25 Interior Partitioning Authority

Within the trim-line interior envelope of an SCU or consolidated SCU assembly, manufacturers may partition interior space in any arrangement consistent with declared habitat class and intended function.

#### 102.26 Operational Protection Limits

No interior partitioning arrangement shall compromise the operation, seal integrity, or emergency accessibility of required sheer doors.

No interior partitioning arrangement shall impede normal ship operation, including routine crew movement, service access, life-support maintenance, command response, and emergency transit paths required by the assigned habitat class.

#### 102.27 Shelter-in-Place Function

Each SCU shall be treated as a shelter-in-place survivability cell capable of emergency isolation from adjacent SCUs when commanded by crew, automation, XO authority, or certified shipboard safety logic.

#### 102.28 Sheer-Door Isolation Requirement

Required sheer doors shall be capable of sealing an SCU from any directly adjacent SCU at shared door interfaces to preserve habitat integrity during fire, decompression, contamination, combat damage, or internal system failure.

#### 102.29 Emergency Closure Priority

In certified emergency-seal mode, sheer-door closure has priority over routine transit and shall execute unless explicitly inhibited by higher-order command authority defined by vessel safety doctrine.

#### 102.30 Obstruction-Clearing Closure

Sheer doors shall be rated and installed as obstruction-clearing barriers for emergency sealing. During emergency closure, door systems may exert sufficient force to sever or displace obstructing material, equipment, cargo, or personnel present in the closure envelope in order to complete seal formation.

#### 102.31 Hazard Marking and Interlocks

SCU designs using obstruction-clearing sheer doors shall provide conspicuous hazard marking, approach warnings, and pre-seal alerts at each door assembly, and shall include doctrine-defined delay/interlock behavior except where immediate seal is required for survivability.

#### 102.32 Sheer-Door Warning Chime

Each required sheer door shall include an audible warning chime that activates when presence is detected in the closure path or when a person lingers within the marked hazard envelope.

#### 102.33 Presence Trigger Scope

The warning system shall be calibrated so that anything present in the closure envelope, including personnel, cargo, tools, debris, or unidentified matter, may trigger the warning alarm.

#### 102.34 Training and Incident Record Value

Warning-chime events should be retained in shipboard incident records as crew-safety training signals.

#### 102.35 Mandatory Shelter-in-Place Drills

All ships operating SCU-based habitats shall conduct shelter-in-place functionality drills in accordance with the maintenance schedule established for their ship class.

#### 102.36 Drill Scope

Shelter-in-place drills shall include verification of sheer-door operation, environmental isolation integrity, EIP supply continuity, and crew response procedures for all certified SCU habitats on the vessel.

#### 102.37 Maintenance Schedule Integration

The frequency and scope of shelter-in-place drills shall be defined by the ship-class maintenance doctrine and enforced by the commanding Warden and the vessel's certification authority.

#### 102.38 Design Compile Pass Requirement

Certified ship design programs shall execute compile passes at defined checkpoints during design development. Compile passes shall map and verify known routing of cables, conduits, and sensor-node networks across all SCU habitats included in the declared ICS.

#### 102.39 Compile Data Scope

Each compile pass shall, at minimum, identify route continuity, node addressing, interface dependencies, service-domain conflicts, and unresolved routing collisions that could impair certification, survivability, or maintenance access.

#### 102.39A Maneuvering-Jet Placement Function

Compile passes shall also evaluate and validate maneuvering-jet placement across the hull and spine, including thrust-vector coverage, control authority distribution, and local structural loading at jet mount locations.

#### 102.39B Mainframe Jet Clustering Logic

The ship mainframe may cluster maneuvering jets into coordinated control groups to reduce maneuvering stress on the SCU spine and connected structural frames. Jet-cluster assignments produced by a compile pass shall be recorded as controlled design outputs.

#### 102.39C Revalidation Requirement for Every Compile

Every compile pass shall revalidate maneuvering stress, structural load margins, routing integrity, and other declared engineering requirements against the current design state. This requirement applies to all hull classes, including large-hull vessels where jet populations may reach the hundreds.

#### 102.40 First Compile Trigger

Upon finalization of ICS documentation, the first compile shall be executed before continuation to downstream design progression, fabrication release, or integration authorization.

#### 102.41 First Compile Certification Authority

The first compile shall be reviewed and certified by a USO engineer authorized for shipboard systems compliance.

#### 102.42 Continuation Constraint

No program may proceed beyond the ICS-complete gate until first-compile certification is recorded in the project compliance file.

## Chapter 2. ILH Layer and Post-ILH Compile Gate

#### 102.43 Interior Laminated Hull (ILH) Definition

Following ICS finalization and first-compile certification, vessels shall implement an Interior Laminated Hull (ILH) as a continuous protective lining applied over ICS habitat boundaries.

#### 102.43A ILH Terminology Normalization

For all code, compile, certification, and maintenance uses, Interior Laminated Hull shall be designated ILH.

The alternate acronym LIH shall be treated as a legacy synonym and shall be normalized to ILH in current and future certification records.

#### 102.44 ILH Material and Layering

The ILH shall be constructed as a multi-layer flexible laminate using certified aramid-class or equivalent kevlar-like protective materials, installed as conforming wraps or cast-form shells around SCU habitat envelopes.

#### 102.44A Sheet Size and Composition Variants

ILH materials may be supplied in manufacturer-defined sheet sizes and composition variants. This code does not prescribe a mandatory sheet dimension standard or a single required laminate composition, provided certified ILH protection performance is achieved.

#### 102.44B Inter-Sheet Bonding Requirement

All adjacent ILH sheets and layer interfaces shall be continuously joined by approved mechanical fastening, approved chemical bonding, or a certified hybrid of both methods so the installed ILH functions as a unified protective envelope.

#### 102.44C Infrastructure Placement and ILH Interface Constraint

All service infrastructure, including but not limited to cable trunks, conduits, sensor trunks, fluid lines, and power distribution runs, shall be routed outside the ILH envelope. The only permitted through-ILH interface shall be a certified manifold connection designed for controlled service transfer across the ILH boundary.

#### 102.45 ILH Primary Function

The sole primary function of the ILH is occupant protection through insulation, spall and debris mitigation, and survivability buffering during decompression, thermal shock, and internal damage events.

#### 102.45A Infrastructure-Protection Exclusion

The ILH shall not be designated, marketed, certified, or credited as an infrastructure-protection system. Protection of cables, conduits, sensor trunks, piping, manifolds, or power-distribution hardware is not an ILH code function.

#### 102.46 Non-Structural Classification

The ILH shall not be classified or credited as a primary structural member of the vessel, and no load-bearing requirement for hull-frame integrity shall be assigned to ILH layers.

#### 102.47 Last-Line Protective Doctrine

Where upstream structural, seal, or systems protections are degraded, the ILH shall be treated as the last internal defensive envelope intended to preserve crew survivability within the ICS.

#### 102.48 Post-ILH Compile Requirement

Upon completion of ILH installation design, a compile pass shall be executed before continuation to subsequent design release, fabrication sequencing, or certification progression.

#### 102.49 Penetration Report Output Requirement

The post-ILH compile output shall include a penetration report identifying every through-ILH interface, penetration type, associated certified manifold reference, seal method, and any unresolved penetration conflicts requiring corrective action.

#### 102.50 Penetration Report Compliance Gate

No project may proceed beyond the ILH compile gate unless the penetration report is complete, traceable to current design revision, and accepted in the project compliance file.

### 102.50A Volume-Normalized Mass (VNM) and Scaling Integration

All vessel designs shall be calibrated against the Volume-Normalized Mass (VNM) standards defined in **AX-004**. 

1. **Linear Scale Factor (k):** The ship compiler shall calculate the vessel's linear scale factor ($k$) relative to the Falcon-class baseline ($L=34.75m$). This factor shall be used to derive all secondary system requirements, including thermal capacity, power output, and engine volume.
2. **Structural Volume Verification:** The compiler shall verify that the gross hull envelope provides sufficient internal volume for the following mandatory ratios:
   - **Thermal Battery Volume ($V_{bat}$):** Not less than 4x the volume of the class-equivalent FTL engine ($V_{eng}$).
   - **Machinery Enclosure:** FTL activation and CDAP generation systems shall occupy a contiguous engineering citadel protected by ISH/ESH layers.

### 102.50B Surface Area and Rejection Infrastructure (Radiator Trunks)

To comply with the thermal rejection laws in **CX-016**, every hull shall provide a minimum **Effective Rejection Area ($A_{ship}$)**.

1. **Radiator Trunk Placement:** Drag-tolerant heat-exchanger trunks shall be integrated into the ESH layer as armored, recessed, or longitudinal panels.
2. **Minimum Rejection Area:** The total area of installed radiator trunks shall satisfy the cooling rate ($Q_{cool}$) required for the ship's class-normalized mass:
   $Q_{cool} \ge q_A \cdot A_{ship}$
3. **Obstruction Doctrine:** Radiator trunks shall be placed to ensure that 90% of their radiant face remains unobstructed by external mounts, weapons, or docked craft during standard FTL cooldown windows.

### 102.50C Field Geometry and Emitter Occlusion

Hull generation shall prioritize "Field Continuity" for both Intrinsic Coupling Fields (FTL) and Energy Shields.

1. **Patch Overlap Requirement:** Emitters shall be distributed across the ESH so that every point on the hull surface is covered by at least two (2) overlapping ICF bubbles ($R_{red} = 2$).
2. **Occlusion Check:** The ship compiler shall perform a line-of-sight check from every emitter node. Any hull protrusion (turrets, sensor masts, nacelles) that occludes more than 15% of an emitter's patch area shall require the installation of supplemental "Contour Emitters" to close the gap.
3. **Geometric Complexity Limit:** Hulls with high "Fractal Depth" or irregular silhouettes that require more than 1.5x the standard emitter count per $A_{box}$ are non-compliant and must be structurally streamlined.

## Chapter 3. ISH and NHS Layer with Post-NHS Compile Gate

#### 102.51 Interior Structural Hull (ISH) Definition

Following ILH compile acceptance, vessels shall implement an Interior Structural Hull (ISH) as the primary structural beam and frame network that interfaces with SCU assemblies.

#### 102.52 SCU-to-ISH Mount Interface Requirement

SCUs shall include predefined certified mount points for ISH connection. ISH beams and frame members shall engage only approved mount interfaces defined in the SCU specification and recorded in build certification documentation.

#### 102.52A Human-Compatible Navigation Infrastructure

Where engineering conditions permit, the ISH shall provide human-compatible navigation infrastructure including walkable surfaces, harness connection points, ladders, handholds, and any Zero-G navigation aids required for safe crew movement and maintenance access.

#### 102.53 Interstitial Maintenance Access Requirement

The interstitial zone between SCU boundaries and surrounding ILH/ISH installation layers shall be treated as a maintenance crawl space and shall provide not less than 0.75 meter clear width and 0.90 meter clear height along required maintenance routes for human access.

#### 102.53A Small-Craft Exemption

For vessels with a total certified interior count below 10 SCUs, the crawl-space dimensional requirement in Section 102.53 may be suspended where equivalent maintenance access procedures are documented in certification records.

#### 102.54 Capital-Class Access Upgrade

For capital-class and larger vessels, required interstitial maintenance routes shall be upgraded from crawl-space access to standing access, providing not less than 0.90 meter clear width and 2.10 meters clear height for a typical human maintainer.

#### 102.54A Unified Hull Structure (UHS)

For shuttlecraft, fighters, utility ships, and other small craft where separate ILH, ISH, and exterior structural hull layers are not practicable, those hull functions may be combined into a single unified layer designated the Unified Hull Structure (UHS).

#### 102.54B UHS Spacing Exemption

Vessels constructed as UHS assemblies shall be exempt from the interstitial spacing requirements in Sections 102.53, 102.53A, and 102.54, provided the unified hull remains certified for its assigned occupancy, survivability, and maintenance doctrine.

#### 102.54C Non-Habitable Hull Space (NHS)

The space between the Interior Structural Hull (ISH) and the Exterior Structural Hull (ESH) shall be designated the Non-Habitable Hull Space (NHS).

The NHS shall be treated as a non-habitable service volume used for automated and maintenance infrastructure, including turret ammunition feeds, power distribution, armor plating storage and transfer, and other ship systems that are not intended for crew occupancy.

#### 102.54D Atmospheric Responsive Foam (ARF)

The exposed boundaries of the NHS shall be coated with Atmospheric Responsive Foam (ARF).

When exposed to oxygen, ARF shall rapidly harden into a rubbery sealing material.

When exposed to vacuum, ARF shall rapidly expand into a similar but less dense rubbery sealing material.

ARF shall function as a temporary hull-breach seal and as a visible indicator of hull compromise.

#### 102.54E ARF Scent Doctrine

ARF may be scented at manufacturer discretion, but any applied scent shall remain unpleasant by code so that breach conditions are readily detectable by crew alertness and response doctrine.

#### 102.54F NHS Access Requirement

Access to the NHS shall be provided through an airlock only.

#### 102.54G NHS Atmosphere Standard

The NHS shall be maintained under inert-gas pressurization.

#### 102.54H NHS Rated Environmental Suit

Personnel entering the NHS shall be issued an NHS rated environmental suit. No additional atmospheric protection shall be required beyond the NHS rated environmental suit unless otherwise mandated by emergency doctrine.

#### 102.54I NHS Top-Off Station Spacing

NHS human atmospheric top-off stations shall be installed at approximately 10 meter intervals as structural elements afford.

#### 102.54J Airlock Suit Staging Requirement

All airlocks providing transit to the ESH shall provide staging capacity for not fewer than 4 NHS rated environmental suits and not more than 6 NHS rated environmental suits, as defined by ship-class certification doctrine.

Each such airlock shall also provide not fewer than 2 EVA class suits.

Shuttlecraft shall provide exactly 6 NHS rated environmental suits and not fewer than 2 EVA class suits, including configurations that do not use a conventional airlock architecture.

#### 102.54K Suit Endurance Requirement

Each NHS rated environmental suit shall provide not less than 1 hour of serviceable use before recharge is required.

#### 102.54L Airlock Top-Off Station Requirement

Each NHS airlock shall include a human atmospheric top-off station within the airlock itself.

#### 102.54L.1 ESH-Transit Airlock Decontamination Requirement

All airlocks providing transit to the ESH shall include decontamination facilities suitable for personnel, suits, and carried equipment returning from exterior operations.

#### 102.54L.2 Sequester Waiting Requirement

All airlocks providing transit to the ESH shall include a waiting area sized for required sequester hold times as determined by mission circumstance, contamination risk, and shipboard medical or command doctrine.

#### 102.54L.3 Optional Extended Processing Facilities

ESH-transit airlocks may include additional support facilities, including sample-intake stations, quarantine processing spaces, and triage support spaces.

#### 102.54M Post-NHS Compile Requirement

Upon completion of NHS airlock placement and top-off station layout, a compile pass shall be issued before further design progression, fabrication release, or certification continuation.

#### 102.54N Engineering Signoff Requirement

The post-NHS compile shall require engineering signoff confirming that all NHS top-off stations comply with spacing and functional requirements, and that all NHS airlock locations are correctly assigned, accessible, and certified for service.

## Chapter 4. ESH Layer and Exterior Identity Marking

#### 102.55 Exterior Structural Hull (ESH) Definition

The Exterior Structural Hull (ESH) shall be the outer structural frame and shell system providing standardized external integration points for ship systems and protective assemblies.

#### 102.56 Standard ESH Mount Classes

ESH designs shall include predefined standardized mount points classified as Heavy, Medium, and Light.

#### 102.57 Utility Mount Points

ESH designs shall also include utility mount points for minor and distributed system integration.

The ship compiler shall be authorized to assign utility mount points for radiators, positional thrusters, sensor assemblies, and other comparable minor system components according to certified routing and stress constraints.

#### 102.58 Ablative Scale Support Infrastructure

All ESH implementations, regardless of ship class, shall provide the infrastructure required to deploy, seat, retain, service, and replace ablative armor scales intended to protect the ESH.

#### 102.59 Mount Retraction and Hatch Protection

All standard mount points, including Heavy, Medium, Light, and utility classes, shall provide retraction capability and armored hatch closure so mounted systems can be secured when not in active use.

#### 102.60 ESH Removable Segmentation Requirement

The ESH shall be engineered for in-station maintenance removal in not fewer than 2 sections.

Equatorial clamshell segmentation is recognized as the most common compliant arrangement.

#### 102.61 Compile-Defined Removal Plan and Certification

The ship compiler shall provide a certified removal and reinstallation plan for all ESH sections, including separation boundaries, lift vectors, locking interfaces, and maintenance access sequences.

ESH segmentation and removal planning outputs shall be reviewed and certified by an authorized engineering signoff before maintenance release.

#### 102.62 ESH Section Naming Convention

Each removable ESH section shall be designated using Greek-alphabet identifiers in sequence (Alpha, Beta, Gamma, Delta, and subsequent labels as required).

#### 102.63 USO Ship Identifier Issuance

Upon final compile acceptance and certification completion, a USO ship identifier shall be issued to the vessel as a mandatory certification artifact.

#### 102.64 Exterior Optical Visibility Requirement

The issued USO ship identifier shall be marked on the ship exterior in a form that is clearly optically visible under standard inspection conditions.

#### 102.65 Directional Coverage and Marking Consistency

The USO ship identifier marking shall be visible from cardinal exterior directions and shall also be visibly present on both dorsal (top) and ventral (bottom) exterior surfaces.

Identifier markings required by this section shall be rendered at consistent certified display size across required viewing faces.

#### 102.66 Command Classification Hull Declaration Requirement

Every certified vessel shall declare its command classification on the exterior hull as either Civilian or Military.

This declaration shall be treated as a mandatory legal identity marking and shall be included in certification records and transponder identity metadata.

#### 102.67 Command Classification Visibility and Consistency

The command classification declaration shall be optically legible under standard inspection conditions and shall be displayed at consistent wording and certified display size across required viewing faces.

Any operational change to declared command classification shall require certification update before deployment under the updated status.

#### 102.68 False-Flag Perfidy Prohibition

Knowingly displaying a false command classification, masking required classification markings, or operating under mismatched hull and transponder classification shall constitute false-flag perfidy.

False-flag perfidy shall be treated as a high crime under Imperial and USO enforcement doctrine.

## Chapter 5. Habitat Classification Standards

### 103 Habitat Classification

#### 103.1 Classification Requirement

Every habitat defined in design documentation, procurement specification, fabrication manifest, or certification filing shall be assigned a primary habitat class.

#### 103.2 Approved Habitat Classes

The approved primary habitat classes are Personnel, Industrial, Storage, Command, Office, Environmental, and Specialized.

#### 103.3 Personnel Habitats

Personnel habitats are spaces primarily intended for crew occupancy, endurance, recovery, hygiene, and routine human support.

Typical personnel habitats include berthing blocks, barracks, sanitary cores, galley and mess spaces, and medical rest wards.

#### 103.4 Industrial Habitats

Industrial habitats are spaces primarily intended for production, processing, repair, fabrication, propulsion support, and heavy technical operations.

Typical industrial habitats include engine rooms, fabrication bays, machine shops, processing lines, and repair workshops.

#### 103.5 Storage Habitats

Storage habitats are spaces primarily intended for material containment, staging, inventory control, and secured stock management.

Typical storage habitats include cargo bays, cold storage, munitions storage, bonded vaults, and logistics staging zones.

#### 103.6 Command Habitats

Command habitats are spaces primarily intended for ship command authority, tactical control, mission coordination, and operational oversight.

Typical command habitats include bridge command decks, combat information centers, command briefing pits, and fleet coordination nodes.

#### 103.6A Bridge Habitat Placement

The primary bridge habitat shall be located near the central point of the ship's certified hull envelope to preserve command survivability, routing resilience, and protected access continuity.

#### 103.6B Bridge Egress Redundancy

A bridge habitat shall provide no fewer than 2 egress points.

The required egress routes shall not share the same first connected SCU beyond the bridge boundary.

One bridge egress should normally route toward officer quarters assigned to bridge watch personnel.

#### 103.6C Deck Transition Redundancy

Any deck shall provide no fewer than 2 transitions to other decks.

This requirement may be waived for Class I and Class II vessels where hull scale or mission envelope does not support dual-transition geometry.

#### 103.6D Bridge Department Integration Minimums

For Class III and higher vessels, command-habitat architecture shall provide certified control positions, display surfaces, and mainframe command channels for not fewer than the following departments:

1. Tactical.
2. Stellar Cartography and Navigation (SCN).
3. Helm Control.
4. Communications.
5. Engineering.
6. Environmental Support.
7. Unified Logistics Management (ULM).

Class I and Class II vessels may consolidate two or more department functions into shared stations, provided all listed command functions remain present and certifiable.

#### 103.6E Command Access-Control Requirement

Bridge command systems shall implement role-based access control defining view and control authority by department function.

Access-control assignments shall be retained in certification documentation and shall be auditable in maintenance and incident records.

#### 103.7 Office Habitats

Office habitats are spaces primarily intended for administration, planning, records management, legal process, and non-command staff operations.

Typical office habitats include administrative suites, records offices, personnel offices, contract desks, and planning rooms.

#### 103.8 Environmental Habitats

Environmental habitats are spaces primarily intended to sustain shipboard life-support conditions and manage atmosphere, water, waste, thermal conditioning, and biosafety controls.

Typical environmental habitats include life-support control rooms, atmospheric processors, water reclamation facilities, waste handling plants, and contamination control airlocks.

#### 103.9 Specialized Habitats

Specialized habitats are spaces whose primary function falls outside routine personnel, industrial, storage, command, office, or environmental use, or which require mission-specific fit-out and certification treatment.

Typical specialized habitats include research labs, quarantine wards, diplomatic chambers, cultural sanctums, black-site compartments, and anomaly containment rooms.

#### 103.10 Mixed-Use Habitats

A habitat with multiple intended functions shall be assigned one primary class and may be assigned one or more secondary class labels for planning and compliance reference.

#### 103.11 Classification Persistence

Habitat class assignment shall persist across design, manufacturing, retrofit, and recertification records unless formally reclassified under an approved alteration standard.

## Chapter 6. Module, Combat-Safety, and FTL Integration

### 104 Module Systems

#### 104.1 Module Definition

For the purposes of this code, a module shall mean any specialized starship system that is non-structural in nature.

#### 104.2 Representative Module Types

Module systems include, but are not limited to, weapon systems, specialized sensor systems, propulsion engines, FTL drives, specialized armor or shield systems, power plants, and other certified mission systems.

#### 104.3 Installation Location and Classing

Modules may be installed internally or externally and may be assigned class ratings, integration categories, and duty designations according to certified ship architecture and mission doctrine.

#### 104.4 Mainframe Integration Requirement

All installed modules shall be integrated into the ship mainframe for command, monitoring, fault reporting, and certification traceability.

#### 104.5 Maintenance Log Disclosure Requirement

All installed modules and their active configuration states shall be disclosed in vessel maintenance logs and retained in certification records.

#### 104.6 Swappable Module Allowance

Swappable module systems are permitted, provided each load and unload action is recorded in maintenance logs with module identifier, installation location, timestamp, and authorizing maintenance authority.

#### 104.7 Module Technical Declaration Requirement

All modules sold, transferred, certified, or installed for ship use shall include a technical declaration package.

#### 104.8 Required Performance and Integration Declarations

The module technical declaration package shall include, at minimum, the following:

1. Declared power consumption.
2. Declared heat generation in watts.
3. Declared mount requirements, including internal installation requirements in SCU habitats where applicable.
4. Declared external installation requirements by certified mount class where applicable.
5. Declared license or permit requirements, where relevant to operation, possession, or transfer, including explicit civilian, military, or dual-use designation.
6. Declared ship-class applicability and restriction profile for module installation and operation.

#### 104.9 Required Manufacturer and Provenance Declarations

The module technical declaration package shall also include manufacturer provenance data, including manufacturer name, make, model, location of manufacturing, and the ATEC identifier used in module construction.

#### 104.10 Recordkeeping and Compliance Use

Module declaration packages shall be retained in procurement files, maintenance records, and certification documentation, and shall be made available for USO audit and compliance inspection.

#### 104.11 TEK and DSB Surrender Pathway Requirement

Combat-capable vessels shall include a certified surrender pathway integrating The Emperor's Kiss (TEK) trigger logic, Distress Surrender Beacon (DSB) activation, and command-transition behavior under declared surrender conditions.

Where TEK loss or declared threshold conditions are met, the DSB shall activate and the vessel shall transition to certified surrender state without requiring discretionary tactical command input.

#### 104.12 Surrender-Custody Logging Requirement

DSB activation, surrender-state transition, and any transfer to Imperial Recovery Services custody shall generate immutable event records in the ship maintenance and incident log stack.

Recorded events shall include trigger source, timestamp, command authority context, and current vessel state at transition.

#### 104.13 FTL Safety Declaration Requirement

All vessels installing FTL-capable modules shall declare certified FTL operating envelopes including spin-up conditions, cooldown constraints, interdiction response behavior, and post-jump stabilization requirements.

FTL declaration packages shall include momentum-preservation handling and forced-jump behavior during cooldown windows where such behavior is supported by installed module doctrine.

#### 104.14 FTL Compile Validation Requirement

Compile passes for FTL-capable vessels shall verify that declared spin-up, cooldown, and interdiction behaviors are represented in command logic, power and thermal budgets, and crew safety procedures.

No FTL-capable design shall advance to operational certification where compile output reports unresolved conflicts in declared FTL safety behavior.

## Chapter 7. Ship Class and Mission-Class Supplemental Compliance

### 105 Ship Class Taxonomy

#### 105.1 Classification Requirement

Every certified vessel shall be assigned one primary ship class for construction, licensing, module compatibility, and compile-governance purposes.

#### 105.2 Primary Class Assignment Doctrine

Primary class assignment shall reflect the vessel's dominant architecture, endurance profile, and operational envelope rather than marketing designation.

Legacy single-operator reference hulls shall not be classed at capsule-era mass assumptions where certified crew, endurance, life-support redundancy, and service-access architecture are present. Final class assignment shall be based on crewed loaded mass and infrastructure burden.

#### 105.3 Recognized Class Ladder

The following engineering class bands are recognized for standard certification use. Each vessel shall be assigned one primary class band based on certified loaded mass, crew doctrine, endurance architecture, and systems complexity.

1. Class I, Solo Operator Craft: 1000 to 8000 metric tons loaded mass. This class is reserved for single-pilot vessels with no dedicated crew billets.
2. Class II, Light Crewed Craft: 6000 to 20000 metric tons loaded mass. This class covers compact multipurpose ships with minimal crew support architecture.
3. Class III, Escort and Medium Hull Craft: 16000 to 200000 metric tons loaded mass. This class covers escort, patrol, and medium fleet-support hulls.
4. Class IV, Heavy Hull Craft: 150000 to 25000000 metric tons loaded mass. This class covers heavy combat, command, and large logistics hulls below capital force-projection scale.
5. Class V, Capital Hull Craft: 20000000 to 9999999000 metric tons loaded mass. This class covers capital force-projection and strategic fleet-core hulls.
6. Class VI, Megastructure Hull Craft: 10000000000 metric tons loaded mass and above. This class covers strategic habitation, migration, ark, and world-scale constructs.

Heavy logistics and extraction hulls, including large transport and mining-barge families, shall be classed by loaded mass and operational infrastructure burden, and may fall into Class IV or higher where engineering requirements warrant.

#### 105.3A Mass-to-SCU Proxy Ratio and Class Examples

For continuity with SCU-based planning models, class compiles shall apply a nominal proxy ratio of 1 SCU physical proxy equivalent per 1000 metric tons of certified loaded ship mass.

This ratio is a classification and planning proxy, not an interior habitable SCU declaration.

Overlap between adjacent class bands is permitted where mission profile, module package, and certification doctrine justify the selected class.

1. Class I, Solo Operator Craft examples: Drone craft, Interceptor, Fighter craft, Strike-fighter craft, Bomber craft, Stealth bomber.
2. Class II, Light Crewed Craft examples: Shuttlecraft, Utility craft, Courier craft, Corvette, Patrol corvette, Gunship, Mining frigate.
3. Class III, Escort and Medium Hull Craft examples: Frigate, Expedition frigate, Assault frigate, Electronic attack frigate, Logistics frigate, Destroyer, Tactical destroyer, Interdictor, Command destroyer, Cruiser, Light cruiser, Heavy cruiser, Heavy assault cruiser, Recon cruiser, Heavy interdictor cruiser, Logistics cruiser, Strategic cruiser, Industrial ship.
4. Class IV, Heavy Hull Craft examples: Battlecruiser, Attack battlecruiser, Command battlecruiser, Battleship, Black-ops battleship, Marauder battleship, Transport ship (including blockade runner and deep-space transport variants), Mining barge, Exhumer, Industrial command ship, Freighter, Jump freighter.
5. Class V, Capital Hull Craft examples: Dreadnought, Carrier, Fleet carrier, Force auxiliary, Supercarrier, Titan-class capital ship.
6. Class VI, Megastructure Hull Craft examples: Colony ship, Mobile station or habitat ship, Generation ship, Ark ship, World ship.

#### 105.4 Multi-Role and Hybrid Vessels

A vessel may carry secondary role labels (for example command, logistics, exploration, escort, siege, mining, or salvage) but shall retain one primary class assignment from Section 105.3 for all compliance actions.

#### 105.5 Reclassification Control

Reclassification to a different primary class shall require updated compile records, module compatibility review, and certification authority approval before operational release.

#### 105.6 Mission Class Registry

In addition to primary engineering class assignment, the following mission classes are recognized and may trigger supplemental code requirements:

1. Scout and Recon Scout.
2. Exploration and Deep Survey Vessel.
3. Science Vessel.
4. Hospital and Medical Support Ship.
5. Troop Transport and Assault Transport.
6. Dropship and Landing Craft.
7. Boarding and Breaching Craft.
8. Escort Carrier and Light Carrier.
9. Arsenal Ship and Missile Boat.
10. Repair Tender and Fleet Tender.
11. Fuel Tanker and Replenishment Ship.
12. Tug and Recovery Tug.
13. Monitor Gun Platform.
14. Passenger Liner and Civil Transport.
15. Diplomatic Yacht and Priority Courier.
16. Detention and Prison Ship.

#### 105.7 Supplemental Compliance Trigger

Where a vessel is assigned one or more mission classes from Section 105.6, compile passes and certification reviews shall apply the supplemental code requirements associated with each assigned mission class.

#### 105.8 Multi-Mission Stack Handling

Vessels assigned multiple mission classes shall satisfy all applicable supplemental requirements, and unresolved conflicts between mission requirements shall be adjudicated by certification authority before operational release.

#### 105.9 Mission-Class Supplemental Requirement Matrix

Unless superseded by a class-specific doctrine addendum, the following minimum supplemental requirements apply when the associated mission class is assigned:

1. Scout and Recon Scout: Enhanced sensor suite capacity, low-signature profile controls, and extended telemetry recording retention.
2. Exploration and Deep Survey Vessel: Dedicated sample-intake and quarantine processing capacity, anomaly-safe data isolation, and recoverable away-team staging support.
3. Science Vessel: Certified lab containment zones, contamination-isolation routing, and artifact custody vaulting for hazardous research material.
4. Hospital and Medical Support Ship: Expanded medical triage capacity, isolation wards, and redundant environmental controls for patient survivability.
5. Troop Transport and Assault Transport: Embarkation and debarkation flow controls, secured personnel staging, and emergency casualty-processing throughput.
6. Dropship and Landing Craft: Repeated atmospheric cycle tolerance, rapid egress hatch sequencing, and shock-rated occupant restraint systems.
7. Boarding and Breaching Craft: Controlled breaching equipment stowage, hostile-atmosphere ingress support, and compartment recapture safety controls.
8. Escort Carrier and Light Carrier: Flight-support control spaces, recoverable craft servicing lanes, and munitions-handling segregation from personnel habitats.
9. Arsenal Ship and Missile Boat: Magazine isolation, blast-route management, and fire-suppression readiness specific to high-yield ordnance storage.
10. Repair Tender and Fleet Tender: Forward maintenance workshop capacity, module swap handling infrastructure, and distributed spare-component staging.
11. Fuel Tanker and Replenishment Ship: Transfer-manifold safety interlocks, spill and vent controls, and segregated ignition-risk boundaries.
12. Tug and Recovery Tug: High-load tow-interface certification, disabled-vessel stabilization controls, and recovery operation command visibility.
13. Monitor Gun Platform: Fire-control hardening, recoil load-path certification, and sustained thermal rejection under continuous firing doctrine.
14. Passenger Liner and Civil Transport: Passenger life-safety egress planning, public-habitat environmental continuity, and civilian emergency messaging systems.
15. Diplomatic Yacht and Priority Courier: Secure communications zones, controlled-access protocol spaces, and tamper-evident records handling.
16. Detention and Prison Ship: Segregated detainee containment architecture, controlled movement corridors, and command-verifiable custody logs.

#### 105.10 Supplemental Requirements Certification Output

For every assigned mission class, compile and certification records shall identify the supplemental requirements applied, the systems satisfying each requirement, and any approved equivalency accepted by certification authority.

#### 105.11 Supplemental Conflict Adjudication Record

Where mission-class supplemental requirements conflict, the adjudicated resolution shall be documented as a controlled certification artifact prior to operational release.

### 105.12 Frame-Specific Volumetric Ratios

The ratio of Interior Conditioned Space (ICS) to Gross Envelope Volume ($V_{env}$) is determined by the specific configuration of the internal spine and modules. The resulting **Density Family** is a descriptive outcome of this internal fit-out (see **AX-004**).

| Density Family | ICS Volumetric Ratio ($V_{ics} / V_{env}$) | Current Reading |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Light** | 0.40 - 0.55 | Carriers, Explorers, Habitat-heavy frames. |
| **Medium** | 0.25 - 0.40 | Standard multi-role and civilian frames. |
| **Heavy** | 0.15 - 0.25 | Armor-dense battleships, industrial bricks. |

The remaining volume in the gross envelope is reserved for the NHS (Non-Habitable Space), ESH infrastructure, armor lattices, and engine-thermal citadels.


## Chapter X. USO Infrastructure Access and Safety Requirements

### X.1 General Safety Provisions

All habitable starship infrastructure and engineering spaces (excluding Tiny-class equipment) shall comply with the following minimum access and safety standards:

1. **Standard Service Clearance:**
   - A minimum of 1.0 meter of unobstructed clearance shall be maintained on all primary service and maintenance sides of major components (FTL engines, thermal batteries, power banks, reactor units, etc.).
   - Secondary sides and non-service faces require a minimum of 0.5 meter clearance unless installed flush to a bulkhead by design.
   - Tiny-class equipment is exempt from clearance requirements.

2. **Access Lanes:**
   - Engineering bays and machinery spaces shall provide at least one continuous access lane, not less than 1.5 meters wide and 2.5 meters high, suitable for manual, forklift, or drone navigation.
   - Overhead clearance for lifting or hoist operations shall be not less than 2.5 meters above the finished deck.

3. **Guard Rails and Barriers:**
   - All elevated walkways, catwalks, and maintenance platforms above 0.5 meters shall be equipped with guard rails meeting USO safety standards.
   - Hazardous zones (moving machinery, high-voltage, thermal risk) shall be marked and physically separated where practical.

4. **Emergency Egress:**
   - Unobstructed emergency egress routes shall be maintained from all points in the engineering bay to at least two separate exits.
   - Emergency shutoff controls and alarms must be accessible within 5 meters of all major equipment clusters.

5. **Lighting and Marking:**
   - All access lanes, service clearances, and hazard zones shall be illuminated to a minimum of 200 lux and marked with high-visibility striping and signage.

6. **Access Platforms:**
   - Where equipment exceeds 2 meters in height, fixed or deployable access platforms, ladders, or catwalks shall be provided for safe maintenance.

7. **Forklift/Drone Access:**
   - Bays intended for heavy equipment handling shall provide reinforced deck paths and turning radii for standard USO forklifts and maintenance drones.

8. **Inspection and Compliance:**
   - All engineering spaces are subject to USO inspection for compliance with these standards. Non-compliance may result in certification suspension or operational restrictions.

*Note: These standards are intended to provide a baseline for safe, maintainable engineering spaces. Ship classes or mission profiles with unique requirements may petition for specific exemptions subject to USO review.*

## Hull Construction and Spine Reconfiguration Doctrine

The canonical process for starship construction under the Unified Starship Building Codes is as follows:

1. **Phase 1: Baseline Design (Envelope Fix):** The initial design phase specifies a baseline engineering spine to define the **Exterior Structural Hull (ESH)**. This locks the vessel's displacement volume ($V_{env}$), regulatory class, and primary surface area. The ESH is registered at this stage.
2. **Phase 2: Custom Configuration (Mass/Density Fix):** Once the ESH is registered, a custom internal spine and module layout are retrofitted into the finalized envelope. 
3. **Density Determination:** The vessel's **Density Family** (Light, Medium, Heavy) is calculated based on this Phase 2 configuration. It is a consequence of the internal mass distribution and armor lattice depth rather than a constraint of the ESH itself.
4. **Lifecycle Reconfiguration:** Internal spines may be swapped or modified throughout the vessel's service life, potentially shifting its Density Family and handling characteristics (VNM) without requiring a new ESH registration.

This doctrine ensures both engineering flexibility and regulatory traceability. All major reconfigurations must be logged in the ship’s record per the Doctrine of Record (CX-002).

## Civilian Ship Classification and Armament Limits

For a vessel to qualify as 'civilian' under USO Unified Starship Building Codes:
- The ship is limited to mounting small arms or smaller weapon systems, regardless of the physical size of its external mounts.
- This restriction explains why a civilian ship may have large or huge external mounts but is only permitted to install small weapons or point defense systems.
- Civilian ships are expected to field point defense for managing incoming debris and basic navigational hazards, but not offensive or military-grade weaponry.
- Any violation of these limits may result in reclassification as a military or paramilitary vessel, with all associated regulatory and legal consequences.
