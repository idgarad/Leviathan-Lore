# CX-012: Settlement, Claims, and Campaign Continuity

Document Status: Draft
Primary Layer: Modern Times
Subject Domain: Doctrine
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This volume defines what happens when a campaign ends and a new one begins. It codifies Imperial liquidation of campaign-scoped holdings, phased settlement release, the pressed-claim alternative, one-campaign continuity limits, and the political logic behind preserving long-horizon identity without allowing inherited frontier empires to snowball unchecked into the next campaign.

## Diegetic Record

Campaigns do not simply close. They are settled. When the Imperial Eye is completed and a territory passes from frontier contest into integrated space, the Throne initiates a formal settlement event across all campaign-scoped holdings. Claims are evaluated, revenues tallied, titles translated into Imperial accounting, and the frontier's temporary order is forced through the empire's continuity machinery.

The default path is liquidation. Holdings tied to the closing campaign are converted into settlement value under Imperial authority. Yet the Throne does not release this value all at once. Immediate total access would allow prior frontier success to overwhelm the next territory before it has properly opened. Instead, access is phased. A small initial portion restores continuity and dignity. The remainder vests over time, preserving longer identity without granting overwhelming opening advantage.

There is, however, a second path: to press a claim. Under this doctrine, a party may forgo immediate liquidation of selected campaign-scoped holdings and instead retain ownership and income rights from those assets into the next campaign for a strictly limited period. The choice is economically real. The claimant sacrifices early liquidity and accepts a temporary handicap in exchange for possible greater return through ongoing dividends, income, or market leverage.

Pressed claims do not create endless dynastic carryover. They survive for one subsequent campaign only. At the next campaign boundary, the choice must be made again from current value rather than inherited myth. This is how the empire keeps continuity alive without permitting infinity of accumulation.

The system is cynical and fair in the distinctly Imperial sense. It preserves the memory of success, but not the right to enter every new frontier already enthroned above it.

## Analytical Breakdown

### Layer Function

- Modern Times: This is a primary modern-layer doctrine. It describes the empire's current method for translating campaign outcomes into long-horizon continuity.
- Ancient History: The doctrine reflects memory of how unchecked inheritance and entrenched surplus can freeze political development.
- Hyborean Era: It echoes the setting's recurring theme that inheritance must be mediated rather than trusted absolutely.
- Eldritch Layer: Not a direct source, but part of the wider setting pressure that no single historical outcome should be allowed to harden into unquestioned permanence.

### Origins

Campaign continuity doctrine emerges from the need to reconcile two competing truths. Frontier success must matter, or campaigns become disposable theater. But if every campaign's full economic dominance can immediately roll into the next one, the frontier ceases to function as a real proving ground.

### Development

The modern settlement doctrine includes several standard features.

- Campaign closure is triggered by completion of the Imperial Eye.
- Default settlement liquidates campaign-scoped holdings into Imperial value.
- Initial access is partial, with the remainder vesting over a defined period.
- Pressed claims allow selected holdings to continue paying into the next campaign instead of liquidating immediately.
- Pressed claims expire after one subsequent campaign unless replaced by a new continuity choice at the next boundary.

### Present Understanding

The current continuity system is understood as a balance between parity and memory.

- Early campaign parity is protected by limiting immediate access to accumulated wealth.
- Long-term identity is protected by preserving eventual access to settlement value and by allowing one-campaign claim continuity.
- Shares, dividends, and archival claims remain politically meaningful because campaign outcomes are not wiped clean even when frontier conditions reset.
- The Throne guarantees the continuity framework so that its economic memory remains credible across campaign boundaries.

## Material Implications

- Campaign endings remain narratively important because holdings, value, and prestige survive in mediated form.
- The empire's political economy feels more credible because it has a doctrine for continuity rather than a pure reset.
- Claims create meaningful strategic differences between conservative liquidation and long-view income retention.
- The doctrine reinforces the setting's post-grimdark identity by showing an empire serious about both fairness and long-duration consequence.
- Houses, corporations, and Wardens all gain reason to think in campaign sequences rather than isolated frontier episodes.

## Author Notes

Purpose: This volume formalizes the empire's economic and legal treatment of campaign endings.

Reasoning: The campaign model in CX-006 needed a corresponding continuity doctrine. Without one, either frontier success would vanish unrealistically or inherited advantage would dominate every new opening. This volume provides the middle path.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Modern Times: Primary location of settlement law, escrow logic, and claim continuity.
- Ancient History: Supplies the cautionary background against letting accumulated dominance become structurally permanent.
- Hyborean Era: Reinforces the setting's recurring suspicion that inheritance without mediation breeds decadence or collapse.
- Eldritch Layer: Indirectly supports the theme that no order should treat itself as final or beyond revision.

Math And Constraint Checks: If settlement value is phased rather than immediate, later volumes need consistent release schedules, escrow rules, and enforcement institutions. If pressed claims can remain productive for one additional campaign, later volumes need realistic assumptions about dividend flow, asset tracing, and claim abuse prevention. If the Throne guarantees these settlements, later volumes need a financial apparatus large and disciplined enough to make that guarantee credible.

FTL Hand-Waves: None introduced here.

Open Questions: What exact vesting schedule is canonically standard; what categories of asset may be pressed rather than liquidated; what conditions void a pressed claim before the next campaign closes; what public justifications does the Throne offer for phased access.
