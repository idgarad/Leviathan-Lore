## Ship Identification Protocols

There are three primary methods for ship identification:

1. **Transponder:**
	- Ships broadcast a digital transponder code, which can be read at long range.
	- Transponders can be forged or spoofed. It is a war crime for a civilian ship to forge a military transponder, or for a military ship to forge a civilian transponder (especially to commit perfidy).
2. **Optical Ship Registry:**
	- Ships display their registry number and name painted on the hull.
	- On known hull classes, registry markings are in fixed, standardized locations and, on civilian ships, should be self-illuminated for visibility.
	- This can be faked with paint or decals, and is vulnerable to damage or deliberate alteration.
3. **Registry Embossment:**
	- The registry is also physically embossed into the hull.
	- While harder to fake, embossments can be forged with effort, and are vulnerable to pitting, battle damage, or deliberate defacement.

Each method has weaknesses, and positive identification often requires cross-checking multiple sources.

### Legal and Tactical Implications
- For military engagement, positive identification is required before firing on a ship to avoid friendly fire and war crimes.
- There is no "optical cloaking"—cloaked ships evade sensors, but can still be detected by optical, LIDAR, or triangulation methods.
- Locking weapons on an unidentified ship (especially a civilian) is a serious offense and may constitute a war crime.
- Smugglers, pirates, and infiltrators frequently forge transponders and registry markings to evade detection or conduct false-flag operations.

Identification and initiative are critical in combat: identifying a target is as important as locking it, and misidentification can have severe legal and tactical consequences.
# Fundamental Constraint

**There are no windows in space.**

All external observation, navigation, and targeting is performed via sensor systems. Visual, infrared, radar, lidar, gravimetric, and other sensor modalities are used to construct a synthetic view of the environment for the crew. Direct line-of-sight through physical windows is neither practical nor safe in the operational context of Leviathan vessels.

## Sensor Engagement Sequence

### Drone Deployment Speed

- For initial calibration, sensor drones (LIDAR and camera types) are assumed to have a maximum speed of 1 km/s.
- This value can be used to estimate the time required for drones to reach optimal imaging positions, based on the distance to and size of the target object.
- This speed is subject to future adjustment as system balance and gameplay needs evolve.

When an unexpected object or hazard appears, especially at close range, the following sequence and constraints apply:

1. **Initial Detection:**
	- The CAS may trigger a collision warning even if no INC was previously present, indicating a new or transient hazard.
	- The system does not reveal the nature of the object—only that a collision risk exists.
2. **Sensor Modality Limits:**
	- Long-range sensors (radar, gravimetric, etc.) are ineffective at very close range due to feedback, static, and blind spots.
	- Only high-resolution, short-range sensors (LIDAR, camera drones) can resolve objects at close range, and require time to deploy and orient.
3. **Sensor Lag and Uncertainty:**
	- Initial sensor returns may be noisy, incomplete, or “smeared” due to object motion (e.g., rotation) or poor geometry.
	- Drones or sensor platforms must maneuver to optimal positions and synchronize with the object’s movement to build a usable image or model. This process takes several seconds.
	- During this period, the crew has only partial or ambiguous information.
4. **Visual Confirmation:**
	- Camera drones or other visual platforms may be deployed for direct imaging, but require time to reach position and match object movement.
	- If the object moves or vanishes before confirmation, the crew may be left with only partial data and unanswered questions.

## Sensor Uncertainty Protocols

- Even with advanced technology, there are moments of “sensor fog” where the crew cannot immediately identify or classify a nearby object.
- Sensor-driven uncertainty is a normal part of operations: ambiguous returns, delayed identification, and incomplete data are expected in close-range or high-interference scenarios.
- The system prioritizes safety (collision avoidance) over identification; the crew may never learn the true nature of a transient hazard.
