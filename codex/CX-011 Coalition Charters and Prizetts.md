# CX-011: Coalition Charters and Prizetts

Document Status: Draft
Primary Layer: Modern Times
Subject Domain: Faction
Audience: Developers | Authors | Readers

## Abstract

This volume defines coalitions: temporary alliances of Wardens serving under the same House during a campaign. It explains how coalitions gain legitimacy through charter, how Prizetts extend personal service to match coalition obligations, how clan doctrine creates political friction inside coalition service, and how support staff and contractors become points of both resilience and espionage risk.

## Diegetic Record

The Warden order is built on contract, but the contract of an individual is not always enough to meet the demands of a campaign. For this reason the empire permits a secondary structure beneath House command and above the single Warden: the coalition.

A coalition is not a clan and not a permanent military estate. It is a pact among Wardens already in the service of one House, formalized through a House contract that defines objective, scope, and duration. Without that charter the coalition is only an intention. With it, the coalition becomes an operational body recognized by House administration and legible to Imperial record.

This arrangement allows Houses to scale action without turning coalition members into a standing sovereign force. Expeditionary detachments, suppression teams, convoy screens, frontier defense circles, and rapid strike formations may all be built through coalition service. They are temporary by design. Their power comes from alignment of contract, not from inheritance.

The Prizett exists because coalition time and personal contract time do not always align. A Warden whose base service term would expire before the coalition's charter concludes may request extension for the duration of the common obligation. This extension is not automatic. It is granted at House discretion and signals that the coalition matters enough to override ordinary contract lapse.

Coalitions also expose the fault lines inside the Warden system. Members may come from different clans with different doctrines, rivalries, or historical grievances. Success can produce new inter-clan legitimacy. Failure can produce diplomatic fallout, expulsion, or generational resentment. Coalitions therefore function both as force multipliers and as political tests.

No coalition survives without support labor. Logistics officers, intelligence clerks, scanner managers, quartermasters, and contract staff make these temporary bodies operational. That same staffing makes them vulnerable to bribery, infiltration, sabotage, and quiet compromise. A coalition that cannot defend its administrative organs may lose long before its first fleet engagement.

## Analytical Breakdown

### Layer Function

- Modern Times: This is a primary modern-layer institution. It describes an operational structure unique to the current feudal-contract order.
- Ancient History: The need for temporary composite forces arises from the empire's distrust of permanent military monopoly.
- Hyborean Era: Clan prestige and inherited ritual codes make coalition politics more complicated than simple modern bureaucracy.
- Eldritch Layer: Not a source of coalition structure, but one reason cross-clan exploratory or suppression teams may be urgently assembled under temporary authority.

### Origins

Coalition structure grows naturally out of the Warden system. Once command is contractual and distributed, the empire requires a lawful means by which multiple Wardens may bind themselves to a common task without becoming a new landed power.

### Development

The modern coalition system hardened around several principles.

- Coalition legitimacy depends on House contract.
- All members must be in active service to the same House when the charter is executed.
- Shared scope, duration, and objective are formally recorded.
- Prizetts extend individual service to preserve coalition integrity when needed.
- Coalition staffing must include support and administrative functions, not only ship commanders.

### Present Understanding

Coalitions are now understood as temporary operational compacts.

- They allow Houses to scale tactical effort without creating permanent sub-sovereign militaries.
- They are useful for frontier assaults, scanner defense, convoy coordination, cult suppression, and multi-Warden campaigns.
- They create bridges or fractures between clans depending on outcome.
- Their support staff create major espionage and counterintelligence vulnerabilities.

## Material Implications

- Coalition service allows the setting to support temporary, high-intensity alliances without breaking the broader Warden neutrality model.
- Prizetts make contract time itself a political tool rather than mere paperwork.
- Clan doctrine remains culturally consequential because coalition service can trigger loyalty conflicts or new diplomatic alignments.
- Support labor matters at the same scale as fleet command, reinforcing the setting's belief that administration is part of warfare rather than an afterthought.
- Coalitions provide a strong framework for campaign-focused stories about trust, prestige, betrayal, and factional improvisation.

## Author Notes

Purpose: This volume gives coalition service a formal place in the setting instead of leaving it as an informal gameplay convenience.

Reasoning: Once the Warden system exists, temporary multi-Warden structures become inevitable. This volume keeps those structures contractual and politically legible instead of letting them blur into undeclared private militaries.

Layer Function Checkpoints:
- Modern Times: Primary location of coalition law, service extension, and operational use.
- Ancient History: Explains why the empire prefers temporary alliance structures over permanent subordinate war-states.
- Hyborean Era: Preserves the importance of clan identity, oath, and inherited rivalry inside otherwise modern contract systems.
- Eldritch Layer: Provides pressure conditions under which unlikely coalition alignments may become necessary.

Math And Constraint Checks: If coalitions can significantly scale House operations, later volumes need realistic assumptions about coalition size, staffing burden, command span, and duration. If Prizetts alter contract timing, later volumes need enforceable legal rules governing when extensions are granted, refused, or abused. If support staff are major espionage targets, later volumes need plausible security, audit, and counterintelligence costs.

FTL Hand-Waves: None introduced here.

Open Questions: What is the largest coalition the empire will sanction before it becomes politically suspect; what grounds most often justify a Prizett; how do clans discipline members whose coalition service violates internal doctrine; what formal privileges does a coalition charter grant beyond ordinary House service.
